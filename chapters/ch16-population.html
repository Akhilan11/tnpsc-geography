CHAPTER 16 / POPULATION

Population & Demographics.

The world's largest population, distributed in startlingly uneven ways across the subcontinent.

OBJECTIVES

  • Recall key population figures: total, growth rate, density.
  • Identify highest and lowest density states.
  • Understand sex ratio, child sex ratio, literacy by state.
  • Recognise the religious and linguistic composition of India.
  • Recall the demographic significance of tribal populations.
  • Understand the demographic transition, age structure, and dividend concept.
  • Distinguish rural-urban migration streams and their causes.

Population is the single fastest-moving variable in Indian geography. Between 1901 and 2011 India's population rose from 23.8 crore to 121 crore — a five-fold jump in a hundred years — and by early 2023 it had crossed 142 crore, overtaking China as the world's most populous country. But the more interesting story is how uneven the change has been: total fertility has fallen below replacement level (2.1) in most southern and western states while it remains near 3.0 in Bihar; life expectancy at birth has climbed from ~ 32 years (1947) to ~ 70 (2024). This chapter is therefore about a moving map — the demographic transition that different regions of India are experiencing at different speeds.

Population — size and growth.

Latest official census of India is Census 2011. The 2021 census was delayed by COVID-19 and is yet to be released as of 2026; UN estimates put India's 2024 population at around 1.43 billion. In April 2023, India overtook China to become the world's most populous country (UN World Population Prospects).

Census 2011 — key numbers

  • Total population: 1,210,854,977 (~ 1.21 billion).
  • Decadal growth rate (2001–2011): 17.7% (a sharp decline from 21.5% in the previous decade).
  • Density: 382 persons / km².
  • Sex ratio: 943 females per 1000 males.
  • Child (0–6) sex ratio: 919 (lowest since independence).
  • Literacy rate: 74.04% (males 82.14%, females 65.46%).
  • Urban population: 31.16%.
KEY FACT — CENSUS HISTORY

The first census attempt was 1872 under Viceroy Lord Mayo (partial). The first synchronous all-India census was in 1881 under W.C. Plowden. Since then a census has been held every 10 years without a break — until Census 2021 was postponed because of the COVID-19 pandemic. As of 2026 the enumeration is expected to be conducted in 2027 (as per government announcements). The Registrar General & Census Commissioner heads the census under the Ministry of Home Affairs.

Density and distribution.

India's average density is 382/km² (Census 2011). This national figure hides one of the largest population-density gradients in the world: from Arunachal Pradesh at 17/km² to Delhi NCT at ~11,000/km², or over 600 times denser. Density is fundamentally a function of water availability, arable land, and historical settlement: the alluvial Ganga basin can support extreme densities because of double-cropped irrigated paddy-wheat; the Himalayan foothills, the Rajasthan desert, and the North-East hills cannot.

  4. Uttar Pradesh — 829/km²

TAMIL NADU CONNECTION — THE DEMOGRAPHIC ACHIEVER

Tamil Nadu (Census 2011): population 72.1 million (6.0% of India). Density 555/km². Sex ratio 996 — among the best in India. Child sex ratio 943. Literacy 80.1%. Urban population 48.4% — among the most urbanised large states in India. Total Fertility Rate: 1.8 (NFHS-5), well below replacement. Life expectancy at birth: 74 years — highest among big states after Kerala. Tamil Nadu is thus considered demographically ahead of India by about 15-20 years. It reached replacement fertility as early as 1993; India as a whole only did so in 2019-21.

Sex ratio.

Sex ratio = females per 1000 males.

States by sex ratio (Census 2011)

  • Highest: Kerala — 1,084 (only state above 1,000)
  • Tamil Nadu — 996
  • Andhra Pradesh — 993
  • Lowest: Haryana — 879. Punjab and J&K low.

Child sex ratio (0-6 years)

  • Lowest: Haryana — 834. Punjab — 846.
  • Highest: Arunachal Pradesh, Mizoram, Meghalaya (> 970).
TNPSC TIP — WHY SEX RATIO KEEPS FALLING IN NORTH-WEST

Sex-selective abortion after the 1980s ultrasound boom was heaviest in prosperous, patrilocal, land-owning states (Haryana, Punjab, western UP). Poverty is not the driver — Kerala, one of India's poorest large states at Independence, has always led on sex ratio. The PC-PNDT Act 1994 criminalises pre-natal sex determination; the Beti Bachao Beti Padhao (2015) campaign combines enforcement with cash incentives. Both are frequently tested.

Literacy.

States by literacy (Census 2011)

  • Highest: Kerala — 93.9%, Lakshadweep — 91.9%, Mizoram — 91.6%, Goa — 88.7%, Tripura — 87.8%.
  • Lowest: Bihar — 61.8% (lowest among states), Arunachal Pradesh — 65.4%, Rajasthan — 66.1%, Jharkhand, Andhra Pradesh.

Tamil Nadu

TN literacy 80.1% (8th highest); male 86.8%, female 73.4%. Urban literacy 87.0%, rural 73.5%.

Religion and language.

  • Hindu — 79.8%
  • Muslim — 14.2%
  • Christian — 2.3%
  • Sikh — 1.7%
  • Buddhist — 0.7%
  • Jain — 0.4%
  • Others / no religion — 0.9%

Religious geography

  • Muslim majority: Jammu & Kashmir (before 2019), Lakshadweep.
  • Christian majority: Nagaland, Mizoram, Meghalaya. Significant in Kerala, TN (Kanyakumari), Goa.
  • Sikh majority: Punjab.
  • Buddhist concentration: Ladakh, Sikkim, Arunachal, Maharashtra (neo-Buddhists).

Languages

The Constitution recognises 22 official languages (8th Schedule). Hindi is the official language of the Union; English is the additional official language. The Census records hundreds of mother tongues, grouped into language families:

  • Indo-Aryan — ~ 78% of speakers; Hindi, Bengali, Marathi, Gujarati, Punjabi, Odia, Assamese, Urdu, Kashmiri, Sindhi, etc.
  • Dravidian — ~ 19%; Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, Malayalam (the "big four"), plus Tulu, Gondi, Brahui.
  • Austroasiatic (Munda & Khasi) — Santhali, Mundari, Khasi.
  • Sino-Tibetan — Bodo, Manipuri (Meitei), Naga, Mizo, Tibetan dialects.

Tamil was declared a "Classical Language" by the Government of India in 2004 — the first language to receive this status.

Scheduled Tribes (ST).

Largest tribal populations by state

  2. Maharashtra
  3. Odisha
  4. Rajasthan
  5. Gujarat
  6. Jharkhand

Highest percentage of tribal population

  1. Mizoram — ~ 94%
  2. Nagaland — ~ 86%
  3. Meghalaya — ~ 86%
  4. Arunachal Pradesh — ~ 69%

Major tribes

  • Gond — largest tribe in India; MP, Chhattisgarh, Maharashtra, AP.
  • Bhil — second largest; Rajasthan, MP, Gujarat, Maharashtra.
  • Santhal — Jharkhand, WB, Odisha, Bihar.
  • Mundas, Oraons, Hos — Jharkhand, eastern India.
  • Naga, Mizo, Khasi, Garo — Northeast.
  • Toda, Kota, Irula, Kurumba, Paniyan — Nilgiri region of TN (Toda is the iconic Nilgiri tribe).
  • Sentinelese, Jarawa, Onge, Great Andamanese — Andaman & Nicobar; among the most isolated peoples.

Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs)

India recognises 75 PVTGs across 18 states/UTs, with pre-agricultural technology, low literacy, declining or stagnant populations. Examples: Sentinelese, Jarawa, Onge (A&N), Toda (TN), Birhor (Jharkhand), Cholanaickan (Kerala), Saharia (MP). The PM-JANMAN scheme (2023) provides ₹24,000 crore for PVTG welfare across housing, roads, drinking water, and healthcare.

Age structure and the demographic dividend.

An age-sex pyramid summarises a country's demographics at a glance. India's is currently "expanding but slimming at the base" — the child cohorts are already smaller than the 20-30 cohort because fertility fell rapidly after 2000. This produces a temporary but powerful demographic dividend: an unusually large share of working-age adults supporting a relatively small dependent population.

Broad age composition (2021 estimates)

  • 0-14 years — ~ 26% (declining fast).
  • 15-59 years (working age) — ~ 65%.
  • 60+ years — ~ 9% (rising rapidly).

Dependency ratio = (children + elderly) / working-age population. India's is at its lowest point in history (~ 47%) and will begin rising again after ~ 2040 as the population ages. The 20-year window ~ 2020-2040 is India's demographic dividend window — the period when demographics themselves boost GDP growth by 1-2 percentage points a year if the workforce is educated and employed.

Median age

India's median age is ~ 28 years (2024), among the youngest of any large economy (China 39, USA 38, Japan 49). Kerala at ~ 33 is already older than the national average; Bihar at ~ 22 is a decade younger.

Demographic transition — the 5-stage model.

The demographic transition theory (Notestein 1945) describes how societies move from high birth and death rates to low birth and death rates in five stages:

  1. High-stationary — high birth rate (BR) & high death rate (DR); slow growth. India before 1921 (the "great divide" year).
  2. Early-expanding — DR falls (public health, food), BR still high; rapid growth. India 1921-1971.
  3. Late-expanding — BR begins to fall; growth continues but decelerating. India 1971-2011.
  4. Low-stationary — BR ≈ DR ≈ low; near-zero growth. Kerala, TN, most of southern India.
  5. Declining — BR < DR; population shrinks. Japan, several European countries.

Different Indian states are simultaneously in stages 3, 4, and 5, which is why regional demographics vary so drastically.

Migration.

Internal migration is enormous: Census 2011 recorded about 45.6 crore internal migrants (~ 37% of population). Largest streams:

  • Rural-to-urban — for work.
  • Rural-to-rural — primarily marriage migration of women.
  • Inter-state — UP, Bihar are net out-migration states; Maharashtra, Delhi, Karnataka, TN, Gujarat are net in-migration.

International: India has the largest diaspora in the world — ~ 18 million Indians abroad (UN 2024). India also receives the highest remittances of any country (~ $120 billion 2023-24).

Population policy.

National Population Policy 2000 — long-term goal to stabilise population by 2045. Immediate targets: universal immunisation, delaying age at marriage, reducing infant mortality below 30, achieving replacement TFR by 2010. India actually achieved replacement-level fertility (Total Fertility Rate = 2.0) in NFHS-5 (2019-21), about a decade behind target but a milestone.

Key schemes since the 2000 policy

  • Janani Suraksha Yojana (2005) — cash incentive for institutional delivery.
  • Mission Indradhanush (2014) — full immunisation for children < 2 & pregnant women.
  • Beti Bachao Beti Padhao (2015) — combat sex-selective abortion; educate girls.
  • Ayushman Bharat (2018) — universal health-care through 1.5 lakh Health-and-Wellness Centres + PMJAY insurance (₹5 lakh annual cover).
  • Poshan Abhiyaan (2018) — reduce child stunting / anaemia.
KEY FACT — HUMAN DEVELOPMENT INDEX (HDI)

India's HDI is 0.644 (medium, rank 134 out of 193 countries, 2024 UN Human Development Report). Kerala tops among states (0.79 — high); Bihar is last (0.57). The three HDI dimensions are: life expectancy at birth, mean/expected years of schooling, and per capita GNI (PPP).
