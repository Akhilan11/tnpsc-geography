CHAPTER 05 / PHYSIOGRAPHY · PENINSULAR PLATEAU

The Peninsular Plateau.

India's oldest, hardest, most mineral-rich land — a triangular tableland that has stood firm since before life crawled out of the sea.

OBJECTIVES OF THIS CHAPTER

  • Understand why the Peninsular Plateau is geologically the oldest part of India.
  • Distinguish the Central Highlands from the Deccan Plateau.
  • Identify the major hill ranges — Aravalli, Vindhya, Satpura, Western Ghats, Eastern Ghats.
  • Compare the Western and Eastern Ghats and explain their climatic asymmetry.
  • Locate the Chotanagpur Plateau and the Meghalaya Plateau and recognise their economic importance.
  • Recall the major peaks of each range with heights.

If the Himalayas are India's youngest and tallest, the Peninsular Plateau is India's oldest and most enduring. While the Himalayas have stood for only about 50 million years, the rocks under your feet in central and southern India are over 2,500 million years old. This part of India was once a piece of Gondwana — the southern supercontinent — and it has remained, more or less unchanged, through every ice age and warm spell since. The plateau is shaped like a triangle, with its base along the Ganga–Yamuna plain and its apex pointing south at Kanyakumari. The Vindhya and Satpura ranges divide it into two: a northern half (the Central Highlands) and a vast southern half (the Deccan Plateau).

Origin and character.

The Peninsular Plateau is a shield — a stable block of ancient crystalline rocks: granite, gneiss, schist, basalt. It is one of the most stable landmasses on Earth, almost never affected by earthquakes (the rare exceptions being the Latur and Koyna events, which occur along reactivated fault lines). The plateau has a general tilt from west to east: most peninsular rivers therefore flow eastward into the Bay of Bengal, except for the Narmada and Tapi which flow westward through rift valleys.

The plateau's average elevation is 600–900 m. The northwest of the Deccan is covered with a thick crust of black basalt (the Deccan Trap) — the lava remnant of one of the largest volcanic eruptions in Earth's history, occurring around 65–66 million years ago, at the end of the Cretaceous. The eruptions are linked to the extinction of the dinosaurs.

KEY FACT — DECCAN TRAP

The Deccan Trap covers about 500,000 km² across Maharashtra, Gujarat, MP, Karnataka, and Telangana. The basalt is up to 2,000 m thick in places. It weathered over millions of years into the famously fertile black cotton soil (Regur) — the backbone of Indian cotton farming.

The Central Highlands.

The northern part of the plateau, lying between the Ganga plain and the Narmada river. Includes:

  • The Aravalli range in the west.
  • The Malwa Plateau in the centre.
  • The Bundelkhand & Baghelkhand uplands further east.
  • The Vindhya range as its southern boundary.

The Deccan Plateau.

The vast triangular plateau south of the Satpura range. Bounded by:

  • The Western Ghats on the west.
  • The Eastern Ghats on the east.
  • The Satpura range in the north.
  • The Cardamom Hills at the south.

Sub-regions: Maharashtra plateau (basalt, fertile black soil), Karnataka plateau (Mysore Plateau), Telangana plateau, Tamil Nadu uplands, and the Chotanagpur plateau (eastern extension into Jharkhand).

The Aravalli Range.

One of the world's oldest mountain ranges (~ 2,500 million years), the Aravallis run northeast-southwest for about 800 km from Delhi to Gujarat, crossing Rajasthan. Once towering, they have been worn down by aeons of erosion. Today the average height is only about 400–600 m, with the highest point at Guru Shikhar (1,722 m) on Mount Abu — the only hill station of Rajasthan.

The Aravalli is heavily eroded over millennia. The famous Aravalli stretch in Delhi (the Delhi Ridge) is its northern extension. Important passes include Dewair Pass and Som-Sirohi Pass. The range is the source of the Banas, Luni, Sabarmati, and Mahi rivers.

The Vindhya and Satpura ranges.

These two parallel east-west ranges divide the Peninsular Plateau into the Central Highlands (north) and the Deccan (south). The narrow valley between them is occupied by the Narmada river — a classic rift valley. South of the Satpura runs the Tapi in another rift valley.

Vindhya Range N of Narmada

  • Runs east-west across MP, parts of UP, Bihar.
  • Average elevation: 600–900 m.
  • Highest peak: Sad-bhawna Shikhar (752 m); Kalumar Peak ~752 m.
  • Eastern extension: Kaimur range in MP/Bihar/UP.

Satpura Range S of Narmada

  • "Satpura" = seven folds; runs east-west across MP & Maharashtra.
  • Average elevation: 900–1,200 m.
  • Highest peak: Dhupgarh (1,350 m) on Mahadev Hills, Pachmarhi.
  • Eastern extension: Maikal Range; source of the Narmada (Amarkantak) and the Son.
  • Other peaks: Astamba Dongar, Mahadeo, Bhanrer.

The Western Ghats — Sahyadri.

Running parallel to India's western coast for about 1,600 km from Tapi river (Gujarat) to Kanyakumari, the Western Ghats — known locally as the Sahyadri — are a continuous, almost unbroken wall. Average elevation: 1,000–1,500 m, rising to 2,000 m+ in the south. They are taller and more continuous than the Eastern Ghats.

The Western Ghats receive the full force of the south-west monsoon. Their western (windward) slope is drenched in rain — Mahabaleshwar, Agumbe, the Mawsynram-equivalent rains. The eastern (leeward) side, the Deccan Plateau proper, sits in a rain shadow.

The Ghats are a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the world's eight "hottest hotspots" of biological diversity. The range is broken at only one major point — the Palghat Gap in Kerala — which is the main route between Kerala and Tamil Nadu.

KEY FACT — ANAIMUDI

Anaimudi (2,695 m) — in the Anaimalai range, Kerala — is the highest peak in peninsular India and the highest peak in any state south of the Himalayas. The name means "elephant's forehead" in Malayalam (and similarly in Tamil).

The Eastern Ghats.

Running parallel to India's eastern coast from Odisha to Tamil Nadu — about 1,750 km — but discontinuous, broken, and considerably lower than the Western Ghats. Average elevation: 600 m. The Ghats are dissected by every major east-flowing river — Mahanadi, Godavari, Krishna, Cauvery — each cutting a gap.

The Western and Eastern Ghats meet in the south at the Nilgiri Hills.

TAMIL NADU CONNECTION — TN HILLS

Of the hills above, TN claims a heavy share: Nilgiris, Anaimalai, Palani, Cardamom (Western Ghats), plus Javadhu, Kalrayan, Shevaroy, Kolli, Pachamalai (Eastern Ghats). We will come back to all of these in Chapter 19, but for TNPSC, knowing which "Ghat family" each hill belongs to is essential.

The Eastern Plateau.

The eastern part of the Peninsular Plateau, extending from Madhya Pradesh through Jharkhand and Odisha to West Bengal. Three sub-plateaus:

1. Chotanagpur Plateau

Spread over Jharkhand, eastern MP, parts of Odisha, Chhattisgarh, and West Bengal. Famously rich in minerals — iron, coal, mica, copper, bauxite. The plateau steps down in three terraces ("Pat" lands). Major rivers: Damodar (the "Sorrow of Bengal"), Subarnarekha, Koel. The plateau is the source of much of India's iron and coal: Jharia, Bokaro, Karanpura, Dhanbad are its coal centres.

2. Baghelkhand

Northwest of Chotanagpur; covers parts of MP and UP. Drained by the Son and its tributaries.

3. Bundelkhand

North of the Vindhyas in MP & UP. Composed of granite gneiss; severely eroded into a "ravine" topography in places.

The Meghalaya Plateau.

A detached block of the Peninsular Plateau, separated from the main plateau by the Garo–Rajmahal Gap. Three hill groups, west to east:

  • Garo Hills — average ~ 1,000 m. Tura is the main town.
  • Khasi Hills — includes Shillong (Meghalaya capital) and Cherrapunji.
  • Jaintia Hills — east of Khasi; Jowai is the main town.

Highest peak: Shillong Peak (1,961 m). The plateau receives the world's heaviest rainfall: Mawsynram (~ 11,872 mm/year) and Cherrapunji (~ 11,777 mm/year) — both in the Khasi Hills.

MNEMONIC — WG vs EG IN ONE GLANCE

WG = Wall — continuous, tall (1,000–2,000+ m), wet on the windward side, broken only at Palghat. EG = Eaten — discontinuous, low (~600 m), broken by every major river crossing east. They meet at the Nilgiris.

QUICK RECAP

What this chapter said.

  • The Peninsular Plateau is India's oldest landmass (~ 2,500 million years), part of the original Gondwana.
  • Vindhya–Satpura divide it into Central Highlands (north) and Deccan Plateau (south).
  • Aravalli — oldest fold range, ~ 800 km, highest peak Guru Shikhar (1,722 m) at Mount Abu.
  • Narmada flows in a rift valley between Vindhya and Satpura; Tapi south of Satpura.
  • Western Ghats (Sahyadri) — 1,600 km, continuous, tall, UNESCO biodiversity hotspot. Highest: Anaimudi (2,695 m). Main break: Palghat Gap.
  • Eastern Ghats — 1,750 km but discontinuous, ~ 600 m. Highest: Arma Konda (1,680 m). Meet WG at the Nilgiris.
  • Chotanagpur Plateau — India's mineral heartland (Jharkhand).
  • Meghalaya Plateau — Garo, Khasi, Jaintia Hills; world's wettest place (Mawsynram).
  • Deccan Trap basalt → fertile black cotton soil (Regur).

Practice MCQs

CH 05 · 10 QUESTIONS
01The highest peak in peninsular India is —
  1. Doddabetta
  2. Anaimudi
  3. Kudremukh
  4. Guru Shikhar
Reveal answer

B — Anaimudi (2,695 m), in the Anaimalai Hills of Kerala.

02The Vindhya and Satpura ranges are separated by the river —
  1. Tapi
  2. Narmada
  3. Mahi
  4. Son
Reveal answer

B — Narmada, flowing west in a rift valley between Vindhya (north) and Satpura (south).

03Guru Shikhar, the highest peak of the Aravalli range, is located in —
  1. Udaipur
  2. Jaipur
  3. Mount Abu
  4. Ajmer
Reveal answer

C — Mount Abu (Sirohi district, Rajasthan). The only hill station of Rajasthan.

04The Palghat Gap separates which two hill ranges?
  1. Nilgiris & Anaimalai
  2. Anaimalai & Cardamom
  3. Anaimalai & Nilgiris
  4. Palani & Cardamom
Reveal answer

C — Anaimalai (south) & Nilgiris (north). The widest gap in the Western Ghats; carries NH-544 and the historic Madras-Calicut railway.

05The Deccan Trap basalt weathered into which famous soil?
  1. Alluvial
  2. Laterite
  3. Black cotton (Regur)
  4. Red & yellow
Reveal answer

C — Black cotton soil (Regur). The basis of Maharashtra's and central India's cotton belt.

06The Chotanagpur Plateau is renowned chiefly for its —
  1. Tea plantations
  2. Mineral wealth
  3. Black cotton soil
  4. Coffee estates
Reveal answer

B — Mineral wealth. Iron, coal (Jharia, Bokaro), mica, bauxite, copper. India's "Ruhr".

07The highest peak of the Eastern Ghats is —
  1. Mahendragiri
  2. Arma Konda
  3. Shevaroy
  4. Doddabetta
Reveal answer

B — Arma Konda (1,680 m), in the Nallamala extension in Andhra Pradesh. (Doddabetta belongs to the Nilgiris, where WG meets EG.)

08Which of the following is not part of the Eastern Ghats in Tamil Nadu?
  1. Javadhu Hills
  2. Kalrayan Hills
  3. Anaimalai Hills
  4. Shevaroy Hills
Reveal answer

C — Anaimalai Hills. These are part of the Western Ghats (Kerala–TN border).

09The Meghalaya Plateau is geologically a part of —
  1. The Himalayan system
  2. The Eastern Hills (Purvanchal)
  3. The Peninsular Plateau
  4. The Northern Plains
Reveal answer

C — Peninsular Plateau. A detached block, separated by the Garo–Rajmahal Gap.

10The Sahyadri is another name for —
  1. Eastern Ghats
  2. Western Ghats
  3. Aravalli
  4. Satpura
Reveal answer

B — Western Ghats.
