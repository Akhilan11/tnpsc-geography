CHAPTER 07 / DRAINAGE · HIMALAYAN RIVERS

Himalayan River Systems.

Three great river systems — Indus, Ganga, Brahmaputra — born of Himalayan snow, fed by Himalayan rain, and the reason half a billion Indians have water.

OBJECTIVES OF THIS CHAPTER

  • Understand why Himalayan rivers are perennial and what makes them different from peninsular rivers.
  • Trace the course of the Indus and its five Punjab tributaries.
  • Trace the Ganga from Gangotri to the Sundarbans; identify all major left and right bank tributaries.
  • Trace the Brahmaputra — Tsangpo / Siang / Brahmaputra / Jamuna — and explain its braided character.
  • Recall the basics of the Indus Water Treaty (1960).
  • Compare Himalayan and Peninsular rivers across multiple dimensions.

Two great river systems were born when the Himalayas rose. The first — older even than the mountains themselves — is the Indus, draining north-west. The second is the Ganga–Brahmaputra, draining south and then east. Together they make up India's three Himalayan river systems. They are perennial: they never run dry, because they are fed in roughly equal parts by glacial melt (summer) and by monsoon rain. In contrast the Peninsular rivers (Chapter 8) depend almost entirely on the monsoon and therefore drop sharply in winter.

The nature of Himalayan rivers.

Five characteristic features:

  1. Perennial — fed by snowmelt + monsoon, so they flow year round.
  2. Antecedent — older than the Himalayas themselves; they cut down through the rising mountains, producing the deep gorges of the Indus and the Brahmaputra (the latter at Namcha Barwa drops through a 5,500-metre gorge).
  3. Long courses — typical lengths 1,500 to 2,900 km.
  4. Carry heavy sediment — the alluvial Northern Plains are entirely their work.
  5. Form deltas at their mouth (Sundarbans is the world's largest).

The Indus river system.

The Indus is the westernmost of India's great rivers. It originates in Tibet near Lake Mansarovar (Kailash range), at about 5,182 m. The Tibetan name is Singi Khabab ("lion's mouth"). It enters India at Demchok in Ladakh, flows north-west through Ladakh in a deep gorge between the Ladakh and Zanskar ranges, then enters Pakistan-administered Kashmir at Chillar, makes a southward turn near Nanga Parbat, and finally empties into the Arabian Sea near Karachi.

Total length: ~ 2,900 km. Length within India: ~ 1,114 km. The system drains ~ 11.78 lakh km² (1.178 million km²), of which about 30% lies in India.

In the Indian section, three small tributaries come from the right (Ladakh / Karakoram): Shyok (joins at Skardu; itself fed by the Nubra from the Siachen glacier), Gilgit, Kabul. The famous tributaries are the five rivers of Punjab on the left bank — north to south:

MNEMONIC — FIVE PUNJAB RIVERS

"J–C–R–B–S" (north to south): Jhelum, Chenab, Ravi, Beas, Sutlej. "Just Crazy Rains Become Sutlej."

The Ganga river system.

The Ganga is India's largest river by water volume, longest river entirely within India, and the central religious-cultural river of the subcontinent. It originates as the Bhagirathi from the Gangotri glacier at Gaumukh (3,892 m), Uttarakhand. The Bhagirathi joins the Alaknanda at Devprayag, and from there is officially called the Ganga.

Total length: ~ 2,525 km (entirely in India and Bangladesh). Course: Uttarakhand → Uttar Pradesh → Bihar → Jharkhand (briefly) → West Bengal → Bangladesh.

Five Prayags (sacred confluences on the headwaters)

  1. Vishnuprayag — Alaknanda + Dhauliganga
  2. Nandprayag — Alaknanda + Nandakini
  3. Karnaprayag — Alaknanda + Pindar
  4. Rudraprayag — Alaknanda + Mandakini
  5. Devprayag — Alaknanda + Bhagirathi → Ganga

MNEMONIC — LEFT-BANK TRIBUTARIES (W → E)

"Ram Gha Go Ga Ko Ma" — Ramganga, Ghaghara, Gomti, Gandak, Kosi, Mahananda. Six left-bank tributaries west to east.

The Ganga delta

In West Bengal, the Ganga splits into two great distributaries: Hooghly (flows past Kolkata to the Bay of Bengal) and the main channel which enters Bangladesh as the Padma. In Bangladesh the Padma is joined by the Brahmaputra (called Jamuna in Bangladesh) and the combined stream meets the Meghna before emptying into the Bay. The resulting delta — the Sundarbans — is the largest river delta in the world, ~ 1,05,000 km² in area, partly in India (~ 4,200 km² in WB) and partly in Bangladesh.

Farakka Barrage

Built on the Ganga at Farakka, West Bengal, in 1975. Diverts water through a feeder canal into the Hooghly to flush silt from Kolkata port. A long-running source of friction with Bangladesh; resolved (mostly) by the Ganga Water Treaty of 1996.

The Brahmaputra river system.

The Brahmaputra is India's most voluminous river (highest discharge), though shorter than the Ganga in its Indian course. It originates in Tibet near Lake Mansarovar (very close to the source of the Indus and Sutlej!) and is called Tsangpo ("purifier"). It flows east through Tibet for about 1,200 km, parallel to the Himalayas, then makes a sharp southward U-turn around Namcha Barwa (7,782 m) — the "Great Bend" — and plunges through one of the deepest canyons in the world into Arunachal Pradesh, where it is known as the Siang (or Dihang).

At Pasighat (Arunachal), the Siang is joined by the Lohit and Dibang and becomes the Brahmaputra. It flows west across Assam for about 720 km, a braided river up to 10 km wide, with countless islands including Majuli. It enters Bangladesh and is called the Jamuna, joins the Ganga (Padma), and reaches the Bay of Bengal.

Total length: ~ 2,900 km. Length within India: ~ 916 km. Annual discharge: about 20,000 m³/s at mouth — among the world's highest.

Major tributaries

Left bank (north): Subansiri, Kameng, Bhareli (Jia), Manas, Tista, Sankosh, Raidak. Most of these are short and steep, falling from the Eastern Himalayas; they carry heavy sediment and trigger Brahmaputra floods.

Right bank (south): Lohit, Burhi Dihing, Dhansiri, Kopili (these come from the Patkai / Naga / Mikir hills).

KEY FACT — A "RAREST" RIVER

The Brahmaputra is one of the very few rivers in the world that is referred to in masculine gender. (The literal Sanskrit name: Son of Brahma.) It is also the only Indian river whose annual discharge during floods can exceed that of the Ganga.

The Indus Water Treaty (1960).

Brokered by the World Bank, signed by Jawaharlal Nehru and Ayub Khan in September 1960. It allocates the Indus system between India and Pakistan as follows:

India's three (Eastern rivers)

  • Ravi, Beas, Sutlej
  • India has exclusive use; ~ 33 million-acre-feet (MAF).
  • Basis of the Bhakra-Nangal, Pong, Ranjit Sagar projects, and the Indira Gandhi Canal.

Pakistan's three (Western rivers)

  • Indus (main stem), Jhelum, Chenab
  • Pakistan has exclusive use; ~ 80 MAF.
  • India retains limited non-consumptive (run-of-river) rights for hydroelectric generation.

The treaty has survived three wars and is considered one of the most successful water-sharing treaties in history. In 2025 India formally announced suspension of the treaty in response to Pakistani support of cross-border terrorism; the legal situation is evolving.

QUICK RECAP

What this chapter said.

  • Three Himalayan systems: Indus, Ganga, Brahmaputra. All perennial, antecedent.
  • Indus rises in Tibet near Mansarovar; total length ~ 2,900 km; Indian length 1,114 km.
  • Five Punjab tributaries of Indus: Jhelum, Chenab, Ravi, Beas, Sutlej (N→S).
  • Ganga: rises as Bhagirathi at Gangotri; Alaknanda + Bhagirathi → Ganga at Devprayag; total 2,525 km.
  • Ganga left-bank tributaries: Ramganga, Ghaghara, Gomti, Gandak, Kosi, Mahananda.
  • Ganga right-bank tributaries: Yamuna (with Chambal, Betwa, Ken), Son.
  • Yamunotri → Yamuna; meets Ganga at Prayagraj.
  • Brahmaputra: Tsangpo (Tibet) → Great Bend at Namcha Barwa → Siang → Brahmaputra (Assam) → Jamuna (Bangladesh). Length ~ 2,900 km. Braided; Majuli island.
  • Sundarbans = world's largest delta; Ganga + Brahmaputra + Meghna.
  • Indus Water Treaty 1960: India gets Ravi+Beas+Sutlej; Pakistan gets Indus+Jhelum+Chenab.

Practice MCQs

CH 07 · 10 QUESTIONS
01The Ganga is officially formed at Devprayag by the confluence of —
  1. Alaknanda & Mandakini
  2. Bhagirathi & Mandakini
  3. Bhagirathi & Alaknanda
  4. Alaknanda & Pindar
Reveal answer

C — Bhagirathi & Alaknanda. Mandakini & Pindar are tributaries that join the Alaknanda earlier (Rudraprayag & Karnaprayag respectively).

02Which of the following is a right-bank tributary of the Ganga?
  1. Gomti
  2. Kosi
  3. Son
  4. Ghaghara
Reveal answer

C — Son. Rises in Amarkantak (peninsular). The others are left-bank.

03Under the Indus Water Treaty (1960), India has exclusive use of —
  1. Indus, Jhelum, Chenab
  2. Jhelum, Chenab, Ravi
  3. Ravi, Beas, Sutlej
  4. Chenab, Beas, Sutlej
Reveal answer

C — Ravi, Beas, Sutlej (the eastern rivers).

04The Brahmaputra is known in Tibet as —
  1. Siang
  2. Tsangpo
  3. Jamuna
  4. Lohit
Reveal answer

B — Tsangpo. Siang is in Arunachal; Jamuna is in Bangladesh; Lohit is a tributary.

05The longest of the five Punjab rivers is —
  1. Jhelum
  2. Chenab
  3. Sutlej
  4. Beas
Reveal answer

B — Chenab (~ 1,180 km), formed by the union of Chandra and Bhaga at Tandi (HP).

06The "Sapt-Kosi" comprises the seven streams that combine to form which river?
  1. Ghaghara
  2. Gandak
  3. Kosi
  4. Tista
Reveal answer

C — Kosi. Drains the Everest–Kanchenjunga region; "Sorrow of Bihar".

07The Brahmaputra makes its "Great Bend" around which peak?
  1. Mount Everest
  2. Kanchenjunga
  3. Namcha Barwa
  4. Kailash
Reveal answer

C — Namcha Barwa (7,782 m), in Tibet.

08The Yamuna originates from —
  1. Gangotri glacier
  2. Yamunotri glacier
  3. Pindari glacier
  4. Milam glacier
Reveal answer

B — Yamunotri glacier (Bandarpunch peak, Uttarakhand).

09The river that is the largest right-bank tributary of the Yamuna is —
  1. Betwa
  2. Ken
  3. Chambal
  4. Sind
Reveal answer

C — Chambal (~ 1,024 km), rising in the Janapao hills, MP.

10The Farakka Barrage is located on the —
  1. Brahmaputra
  2. Ganga
  3. Yamuna
  4. Hooghly
Reveal answer

B — Ganga. The barrage diverts water through a feeder canal into the Hooghly to keep Kolkata port silt-free.
