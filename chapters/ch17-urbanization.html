CHAPTER 17 / URBANIZATION

Urbanization & Settlements.

India is still mostly rural — but it is urbanising faster than at any point in its history.

OBJECTIVES

  • Classify rural settlement patterns (compact, dispersed, hamleted).
  • Understand the Census definition of urban and the six-fold classification.
  • Recall the million-plus cities of India and metropolitan corridors.
  • Know the Smart Cities, AMRUT, HRIDAY, PMAY and Swachh Bharat missions.
  • Identify key urban problems and solutions.
  • Understand India's urban hierarchy and functional typology of towns.
  • Appreciate Tamil Nadu's exceptional urbanisation profile.

India is in the middle of one of the largest urban migrations in human history. Every year about 10 million Indians move to cities — a population equal to the whole of Sweden — and by 2050 the UN projects India's urban population will double from 46 crore (2020) to over 87 crore, with two-thirds of that growth coming in cities that are today smaller than a million people. What is being built in these cities in the next twenty years will decide the shape of India's economy, its politics, and its environmental impact for the rest of the century. This chapter therefore looks at both the settled Indian village pattern that shaped village geography for four thousand years, and the very fast transformation currently underway in urban India.

Rural settlements.

Around 69% of India still lives in rural areas (Census 2011). Settlement pattern is shaped by water availability, terrain, security needs, and culture.

Patterns

  • Compact / clustered (nucleated) — dense, walled village around a temple/well; typical of the Ganga plain, Punjab-Haryana, parts of TN.
  • Semi-clustered / fragmented — a main settlement with peripheral hamlets; Gujarat, parts of Rajasthan.
  • Hamleted — multiple small hamlets within one revenue village; common in Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, lower Himalayas.
  • Dispersed / isolated — homes scattered widely; Meghalaya, Nagaland, Kerala (linear along the backwaters), hill terrain.

Settlement shapes

Linear (along a road or river), rectangular (Punjab grid), circular (around a tank), star-shaped (at road junctions), T- or Y-shaped (at confluences), fan-shaped (at deltas or foothills).

TAMIL NADU CONNECTION — TANK-CENTRED VILLAGES

The classical Tamil village is circular, built around a large temple tank. Every village of the Cauvery-Vaigai delta system was traditionally organised around one or more eri (irrigation tank) that fed the paddy fields — a settlement pattern that survived from Chola times. The tank was simultaneously the village's water supply, drainage sink, groundwater recharge structure, and social space. Tamil Nadu had ~ 39,000 such tanks; roughly 60% are still functional despite urban pressure. The Kudimaramathu scheme (2017-) revives community-based tank maintenance.

Urban settlements — Census definition.

Census of India defines an urban area as:

  1. All places with a municipality, corporation, cantonment, or notified town area; OR
  2. All other places that meet all three of: minimum population 5,000, at least 75% of male main workers engaged in non-agricultural pursuits, and population density of at least 400 persons/km².

Census 2011 recorded 7,935 urban towns (3,894 statutory towns + 4,041 census towns) housing 37.7 crore people (31.16% of total population).

Six-fold Census classification of towns

  • Class I — > 1,00,000 (cities). The 53 cities > 1 million are "million-plus cities".
  • Class II — 50,000 to 99,999
  • Class III — 20,000 to 49,999
  • Class IV — 10,000 to 19,999
  • Class V — 5,000 to 9,999
  • Class VI — < 5,000

Functional classification (Ashok Mitra typology)

  • Administrative — Delhi, state capitals, Chandigarh.
  • Commercial — Mumbai, Kolkata, Kanpur, Bengaluru.
  • Industrial — Jamshedpur, Bhilai, Bokaro, Coimbatore, Modinagar.
  • Educational — Varanasi, Aligarh, Roorkee, Pilani, Kharagpur.
  • Religious / cultural — Varanasi, Madurai, Tirupati, Amritsar, Puri, Haridwar.
  • Tourist / resort — Shimla, Ooty, Nainital, Goa, Kodaikanal.
  • Transport / port — Mumbai, Visakhapatnam, Kandla, Chennai, Kolkata.
  • Defence / cantonment — Pune, Allahabad, Meerut, Ambala, Wellington (TN).
  • Mining — Dhanbad, Singareni, Raniganj, Digboi, Neyveli.
  • Garrison / satellite — Noida, Gurugram, Faridabad, Ghaziabad (NCR).

Urban Agglomeration (UA) and Standard Urban Area (SUA)

Census defines an Urban Agglomeration as a continuous urban spread of a core town plus its adjoining "out-growths" (railway colonies, university campuses, ports outside town limits). A Standard Urban Area is projected to include future growth in a 20-25 year window. Delhi UA, Mumbai UA, Chennai UA are all much larger than Delhi, Mumbai, Chennai city proper.

Rank-size rule

The rank-size rule (George Zipf, 1949) says the population of the n-th largest city in a country ≈ population of the largest city / n. Indian cities roughly follow this pattern in mid-ranks but with a "primate" head — Mumbai and Delhi are somewhat larger than the rule predicts. Countries dominated by one giant city (Bangkok, Manila) are called primate-city systems; India's system is intermediate.

TAMIL NADU CONNECTION — MILLION-PLUS CITIES

Tamil Nadu has the highest number of million-plus urban agglomerations in any single Indian state (along with Maharashtra). Key million-plus cities in TN: Chennai, Coimbatore, Madurai, Tiruchirappalli, Tiruppur, Salem, Erode, Vellore, Tirunelveli, Thoothukudi. TN's urbanisation rate (48.4%) is highest among major states.

Smart Cities Mission & allied schemes.

  • Smart Cities Mission — launched 25 June 2015. 100 cities selected through three rounds of competitive challenge. Cities formulate a Smart City Plan; SPVs implement projects worth ~ ₹2 lakh crore in aggregate. Tamil Nadu has 11 selected: Chennai, Coimbatore, Madurai, Tiruchirappalli, Tirunelveli, Salem, Vellore, Tiruppur, Erode, Thoothukudi, Thanjavur.
  • AMRUT (Atal Mission for Rejuvenation and Urban Transformation) — covers 500 cities (1 lakh+ population); basic infra (water, sewerage, parks, transport).
  • HRIDAY (Heritage City Development & Augmentation Yojana) — 12 heritage cities including Madurai, Kanchipuram, Velankanni, Warangal, Amritsar, Ajmer, Varanasi.
  • PMAY-Urban — Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana — Housing for All target.
  • Swachh Bharat Mission Urban.

Urban problems.

  • Housing & slums — about 17% of urban India lives in slums; Mumbai's Dharavi is among Asia's largest (over 1 million residents in ~ 2.4 sq km).
  • Transport congestion — Bengaluru and Delhi traffic regularly ranked worst in the world by TomTom Traffic Index.
  • Air pollution — Delhi, Ghaziabad, Lucknow, Patna among world's most polluted cities (WHO). PM2.5 in Delhi routinely exceeds 400 µg/m³ in winter — over 25× the WHO limit.
  • Water stress — Chennai's 2019 "Day Zero" crisis when all four reservoirs went dry; Bengaluru summer water shortages; NITI Aayog projects 21 major cities may run out of groundwater by 2030.
  • Solid waste — 1.5 lakh tonnes generated daily nationally; 30% uncollected. India's largest waste mountains: Ghazipur (Delhi), Deonar (Mumbai), Kodungaiyur & Perungudi (Chennai).
  • Urban floods — Mumbai 2005 (940 mm in a day), Chennai 2015 & 2023, Bengaluru 2022, Hyderabad 2020, Delhi 2023. Common cause: filled-up lakes and encroached storm drains.
  • Heat island — urban temperatures 3-5 °C above surroundings, driven by concrete, glass, air-conditioning heat rejection, and reduced tree cover.
  • Groundwater depletion — Bengaluru's water table has fallen 10-15 m in a decade; Delhi's is over-exploited by ~ 130%.

Peri-urban and satellite towns

The zone at the outer edge of large cities, where farmland meets suburban development, is the peri-urban or urban fringe. In India this is the fastest-growing settlement type — often outside municipal jurisdiction, with poor services but heavy real-estate speculation. Examples: OMR (Chennai's Old Mahabalipuram Road corridor), Whitefield & Sarjapur (Bengaluru), Gurugram-Manesar (Delhi NCR), New Town Rajarhat (Kolkata), Navi Mumbai.

Solutions and reforms

  • 74th Constitutional Amendment (1992) — mandated urban local self-government through Municipal Corporations (population > 10 lakh, e.g. Chennai Corporation), Municipal Councils (> 3 lakh), and Nagar Panchayats (transitional areas). Created 18 subject areas devolved to ULBs in the 12th Schedule.
  • Metropolitan Planning Committees for UAs > 10 lakh.
  • Master plans & TOD — transit-oriented development around metro corridors.
  • Sponge cities — porous surfaces, wetlands restoration for flood control.
  • Waste segregation at source under Swachh Bharat; waste-to-energy plants.
KEY FACT — 74TH AMENDMENT & ULBs

The 74th Constitutional Amendment Act, 1992 gave constitutional status to urban local bodies. It set up three tiers — Municipal Corporation, Municipal Council, Nagar Panchayat — and required elected mayors, 33% reservation for women, ward committees, and the 12th Schedule listing 18 functions (urban planning, water, sanitation, fire services, slums, poverty alleviation). State Election Commissions conduct ULB elections. Frequently paired with the 73rd Amendment (rural local self-govt) in MCQs.
