CHAPTER 04 / PHYSIOGRAPHY · NORTHERN PLAINS

The Northern Plains.

The Indo-Gangetic-Brahmaputra plain — the world's largest alluvial flatland, the breadbasket of India, and home to a quarter of humanity.

OBJECTIVES OF THIS CHAPTER

  • Explain how the Northern Plains formed from Himalayan sediment.
  • Identify the three sub-divisions: Punjab, Ganga, and Brahmaputra plains.
  • Distinguish the four relief belts — Bhabar, Terai, Bhangar, Khadar — and their agricultural significance.
  • Name the major doabs of the Punjab and the Ganga systems.
  • Appreciate why this region is India's economic and political heartland.

South of the Himalayas, stretching for about 2,400 km from the Punjab in the west to the Brahmaputra valley in the east, lies one of the world's flattest, most fertile, and most densely populated landscapes — the Northern Plains of India. The plain is about 240 to 320 km wide; the elevation rarely exceeds 200 metres above sea level. It accounts for roughly 1/7th of India's area but supports nearly half its population. This is the land of paddy, wheat, sugarcane, jute; the land where Indian civilisation grew up along the banks of the Indus, the Ganga, and the Brahmaputra.

How the plains formed.

The Northern Plains are a depositional landform. When the Indian plate collided with the Eurasian plate and the Himalayas rose, a long depression — a kind of trough — was left in front of the new mountains. Geologists call this the foredeep. Rivers descending from the still-rising Himalayas carried enormous quantities of sediment, dumping it into this trough. Over millions of years, the trough filled up. The deposits — sand, silt, clay, gravel — are collectively called alluvium, and in places they are several thousand metres thick.

The process is still active. Every monsoon, Himalayan rivers carry millions of tonnes of fresh sediment into the plains and into the sea. The plain itself is dead flat: the entire 2,400 km gradient from Saharanpur (in the foothills) to the Ganga–Brahmaputra delta drops by less than 200 metres — that is the geometric equivalent of a gentle ramp.

KEY FACT — ALLUVIAL DEPTH

In central parts of the Northern Plain, the alluvium is more than 2,000 metres deep. Drilling has shown thicknesses up to 6,000 m in places. This is the foundation of the plain's astonishing fertility.

The three great divisions.

From west to east, the Northern Plains are conventionally divided into three sections, each named after the major river system that built it.

1. The Punjab Plain ~640 km

  • Built by the Indus and its five tributaries — Jhelum, Chenab, Ravi, Beas, Sutlej.
  • Lies in Punjab, Haryana, parts of Rajasthan.
  • "Punjab" means five waters in Persian.
  • Western part is in Pakistan. The Indian portion is mostly the southern half.

2. The Ganga Plain ~1,400 km

  • Built by the Ganga and its tributaries — Yamuna, Ramganga, Gomti, Ghaghara, Gandak, Kosi.
  • The largest sub-division. Lies in UP, Bihar, parts of Jharkhand and West Bengal.
  • Three sub-sections: Upper (UP), Middle (Bihar), Lower (WB / delta).
  • Most densely populated part of India.

3. The Brahmaputra Plain ~720 km

  • Built by the Brahmaputra and tributaries — Tista, Manas, Subansiri, Lohit, Dhansiri.
  • Lies mainly in Assam (the Brahmaputra Valley).
  • The river is braided, with many islands; Majuli is the world's largest river island.
  • Annual heavy flooding because of high discharge and steep tributary gradient.

Total extent DIMENSIONS

  • Length (W→E): about 2,400 km.
  • Width: 240–320 km.
  • Total area: about 700,000 km².
  • Highest elevation: ~ 300 m (Punjab); falls to ~ 30 m at the delta.

Four relief belts from the foothills southward.

As you walk from the foot of the Shivalik southward into the open plain, you pass through four distinct belts — each with a name, a soil type, and an agricultural personality. Memorising them in order (north → south) is a frequent TNPSC favourite.

1. Bhabar

A narrow belt, 8 to 16 km wide, running along the southern edge of the Shivalik. The slope is steep, the rivers are vigorous, and the deposit is coarse — pebbles, gravel, boulders. The streams disappear underground here, only to re-emerge in the Terai belt to the south. Cultivation is difficult because of porous soil and unstable water table. Forest cover is high.

2. Terai

South of the Bhabar. About 15 to 30 km wide. This is where the streams that vanished in the Bhabar re-surface, creating marshy, swampy land. Originally a malarial jungle, the Terai was extensively cleared after independence (especially for refugee resettlement) and is today a major paddy-growing belt. The Jim Corbett National Park (Uttarakhand) lies along the Terai.

3. Bhangar

The older alluvium, deposited away from active river channels. Found in slightly higher ground, often 20–30 metres above the flood plains. Soils are darker, drier, and contain kankar (calcareous nodules). Bhangar lands are less fertile than Khadar because they are not refreshed by new sediment, and in some places they have become alkaline.

4. Khadar

The newer alluvium, deposited every monsoon along active flood plains. Light coloured, fine, highly fertile because of the annual refresh of nutrients. Khadar belts are the most productive agricultural strips in the country — especially for sugarcane, paddy, wheat. The downside: vulnerable to floods.

MNEMONIC — RELIEF BELTS N → S

"B–T–B–K" — Bhabar (pebbles, streams sink) → Terai (marsh, streams re-emerge) → Bhangar (old alluvium, kankar) → Khadar (new alluvium, most fertile). North = young hills, south = old plain — but interestingly the soils in Bhangar are older than the Khadar to its south, because Khadar is refreshed each year.

Doabs — the lands between rivers.

A doab (Hindi do = two, ab = water) is a tract of land between two rivers. The doabs of the Punjab plain are named for the rivers that bound them — a tidy system worth memorising.

Ganga–Yamuna doab

The land between the Yamuna and the Ganga — a wide, fertile, historically important corridor. It runs through Delhi, Mathura, Agra, Etawah, Kanpur, Allahabad (Prayagraj — where the Yamuna joins the Ganga). Most of UP's wheat and sugarcane comes from here.

MNEMONIC — PUNJAB DOABS NORTH→SOUTH

"Si–Cha–Ra–Ba–Bi" (reading north→south = Sind Sagar, Chaj, Rachna, Bari, Bist) — only the southernmost two (Bari and Bist) lie wholly in Indian Punjab.

Regional sub-plains.

Upper Ganga Plain

Western Uttar Pradesh. Covers the area between the Yamuna and the Kosi. Slope is gentle west to east. Sub-features include the Rohilkhand plain and the Awadh plain. Heavy irrigation; sugarcane belt of India.

Middle Ganga Plain

Eastern Uttar Pradesh and Bihar. Heavy summer rainfall, severe annual flooding from the Kosi (called the "Sorrow of Bihar" for centuries before its course was managed). Paddy belt.

Lower Ganga Plain

West Bengal and the Sundarbans delta. The largest delta in the world. The Ganga splits into many distributaries — Hooghly is the main one — and merges with the Brahmaputra in Bangladesh to form the Padma–Meghna system. Tidal forests of Sundari trees give the Sundarbans its name. Home of the Bengal tiger and the largest mangrove ecosystem.

Brahmaputra Plain

An elongated plain along the Brahmaputra in Assam. Bounded by the Eastern Himalayas in the north, the Garo-Khasi-Jaintia hills in the south, the Patkai range in the east, and the Bangladesh border in the west. Majuli (1,250 km² at peak; ~352 km² currently due to erosion) is the world's largest river island and an important Vaishnavite cultural centre. The Brahmaputra here is braided and unstable, shifting course every few years.

KEY FACT — REET vs RUH

The Northern Plains divide into: Bhabar (gravel) → Terai (marsh) → Bhangar (old alluvium with kankar) → Khadar (new alluvium, fertile). In the Brahmaputra valley, equivalent belts are sometimes called Reet and Ruh, but TNPSC almost always uses the standard four names above.

Why the plain matters.

1. The cradle of Indian civilisation

From Harappa and Mohenjo-daro on the Indus, to the Vedic kingdoms on the Ganga, to the Mauryan and Gupta empires, to the Mughal capitals (Delhi, Agra, Lucknow) — Indian political and cultural history was made on this plain.

2. Agricultural powerhouse

Deep alluvial soils, flat terrain, perennial Himalayan rivers and a long growing season together produce ~40% of India's food grains. The Green Revolution was concentrated here (Punjab, Haryana, western UP).

3. Densely populated

The plain houses about 40–45% of India's population on just 17% of its land. Population density is among the highest in the world.

4. Industrial and transport heartland

Easy terrain, navigable rivers, abundant water and labour. The plain is criss-crossed by the densest road and rail network in India (Grand Trunk Road, Golden Quadrilateral, Delhi–Howrah trunk line).

5. Hazards

Floods (Bihar, Assam), waterlogging (Punjab–Haryana from canal irrigation), groundwater depletion, soil salinity. The Bengal and Bihar floods, in particular, are perennial humanitarian challenges.

TAMIL NADU CONNECTION

Tamil Nadu does not have a plain of this scale, but its Coromandel coastal plain (Chapter 6) and the Cauvery delta in Thanjavur–Tiruvarur–Nagapattinam are smaller analogues: alluvial, intensively cultivated, paddy-dominated, dense in population. Thanjavur is to TN what the Ganga plain is to India — the granary.

QUICK RECAP

What this chapter said.

  • The Northern Plains formed by Himalayan rivers filling the foredeep trough with alluvium over millions of years.
  • Length 2,400 km, width 240–320 km, alluvium depth often > 2,000 m.
  • Three sub-divisions W→E: Punjab Plain, Ganga Plain, Brahmaputra Plain.
  • Four relief belts N→S: Bhabar → Terai → Bhangar → Khadar. Khadar is the most fertile.
  • Punjab doabs: Bist, Bari, Rachna, Chaj, Sind Sagar (only Bist & Bari are in Indian Punjab).
  • Majuli in the Brahmaputra is the world's largest river island.
  • The Sundarbans is the world's largest delta and largest mangrove forest.
  • The plain has ~17% of India's area but ~40%+ of its population and food grain output.

Practice MCQs

CH 04 · 10 QUESTIONS
01The Northern Plains have been formed primarily by —
  1. Wind deposition
  2. Glacial activity
  3. River alluvial deposition
  4. Volcanic eruptions
Reveal answer

C — River alluvial deposition. Himalayan rivers deposited sediment in the foredeep over millions of years.

02The narrow belt of pebbles and gravel at the foot of the Shivaliks, where streams disappear underground, is called —
  1. Terai
  2. Bhabar
  3. Bhangar
  4. Khadar
Reveal answer

B — Bhabar. Coarse deposits force streams to percolate; they re-emerge as marsh in the Terai.

03The newer alluvium found along active flood plains is —
  1. Bhangar
  2. Khadar
  3. Bhabar
  4. Reh
Reveal answer

B — Khadar. Refreshed every monsoon, very fertile. Bhangar is the older alluvium on higher terraces.

04The Bist Doab lies between which two rivers?
  1. Beas & Ravi
  2. Beas & Sutlej
  3. Ravi & Chenab
  4. Chenab & Jhelum
Reveal answer

B — Beas & Sutlej. Also called the Bist Jalandhar Doab; lies entirely in Indian Punjab.

05The world's largest river island is —
  1. Sriharikota
  2. Diu
  3. Majuli
  4. Salsette
Reveal answer

C — Majuli (Assam, in the Brahmaputra). A Vaishnavite cultural centre; declared a district in 2016.

06The Sundarbans delta is built by the rivers —
  1. Mahanadi & Godavari
  2. Ganga & Brahmaputra
  3. Indus & Sutlej
  4. Krishna & Cauvery
Reveal answer

B — Ganga & Brahmaputra. The combined delta is the world's largest. The Indian portion is in West Bengal; the larger part is in Bangladesh.

07The Punjab plain takes its name from the Persian for —
  1. Land of five winds
  2. Land of five waters
  3. Land of five lakes
  4. Land of five forts
Reveal answer

B — Land of five waters: Jhelum, Chenab, Ravi, Beas, Sutlej.

08The "Sorrow of Bihar" — a river notorious for shifting its course and devastating floods — is the —
  1. Gandak
  2. Ghaghara
  3. Kosi
  4. Son
Reveal answer

C — Kosi. Originates in Nepal, splits into seven distributaries (Sapt-Kosi); has shifted its course westward by about 120 km in the last 200 years.

09The Northern Plains cover approximately what percentage of India's area?
  1. 7 %
  2. 17 %
  3. 27 %
  4. 37 %
Reveal answer

B — about 17 % (700,000 km² out of 3.28 million km²). Yet supports ~40%+ of India's population.

10"Duars" — the eastern equivalent of Duns — are flat valleys located in —
  1. Punjab & Haryana
  2. Uttarakhand & UP
  3. Assam & northern West Bengal
  4. Madhya Pradesh
Reveal answer

C — Assam & northern West Bengal. The Duars are home to extensive tea plantations.
