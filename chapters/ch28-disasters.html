CHAPTER 28 / DISASTERS & MANAGEMENT

Natural Disasters & Management.

India sits on three of the world's biggest disaster pathways — and has built one of the largest disaster response systems to match.

OBJECTIVES

  • Classify natural disasters.
  • Understand earthquakes, volcanoes, tsunamis with scales of measurement.
  • Recall Indian disaster geography: earthquake zones, cyclone zones, flood-prone areas.
  • Know the structure and laws of disaster management in India.

Classification of disasters.

  • Geological: earthquakes, volcanoes, tsunamis, landslides.
  • Hydro-meteorological: cyclones, floods, droughts, heat waves, cold waves, cloudbursts.
  • Biological: epidemics, pandemics, pest infestations.
  • Industrial / man-made: chemical leaks, nuclear, oil spills, fires.

Earthquakes.

  • Sudden release of energy in the Earth's crust along faults.
  • Focus / Hypocenter: point of release inside earth.
  • Epicenter: point on surface directly above focus.
  • Seismic waves: P (primary, fastest, longitudinal), S (secondary, transverse, only through solids), L & R (surface).
  • Measurement scales:
    • Richter scale — magnitude (energy released); logarithmic; 1-10.
    • Mercalli intensity scale — perceived shaking (I-XII).
    • Moment magnitude (Mw) — modern measure, for large quakes.

India's earthquake zones (BIS, 5 zones revised → 4 zones)

  • Zone V (Very High Risk) — Kashmir Valley, Himachal NW, Uttarakhand parts, NE India (Assam, Arunachal, Manipur, Nagaland, Mizoram), Kachchh (Gujarat), Andaman & Nicobar.
  • Zone IV (High Risk) — Delhi NCR, J&K (parts), Uttarakhand foothills, north Bihar, parts of WB, Sikkim, north MP.
  • Zone III (Moderate Risk) — most of remaining N India, Western Ghats coast, central India.
  • Zone II (Low Risk) — most of peninsular India (much of Karnataka, TN, AP, Telangana, Odisha — but verify with district-level maps).

Major Indian earthquakes

  • 1819 Rann of Kachchh — created the "Allah Bund".
  • 1934 Bihar-Nepal — Mw 8.0+; ~ 11,000 dead.
  • 1950 Assam-Tibet — Mw 8.6.
  • 1993 Latur (Maharashtra) — Mw 6.4; ~ 10,000 dead. Unexpected because it was Zone II!
  • 2001 Bhuj (Gujarat) — Mw 7.7; ~ 20,000 dead. Largest recent.
  • 2005 Kashmir — Mw 7.6, mostly in PoK.
  • 2011 Sikkim — Mw 6.9.
  • 2015 Nepal — affected India (UP, Bihar).
  • 2023 Joshimath subsidence — Uttarakhand; slow geological hazard.

Volcanoes.

  • Three types: active (recently erupted), dormant (not in living memory), extinct.
  • The "Pacific Ring of Fire" hosts ~ 75% of world's volcanoes.
  • India's only active volcano: Barren Island (Andaman). Narcondam Island is dormant.
  • Famous eruptions: Krakatoa (1883, Indonesia), Mt St Helens (1980, USA), Pinatubo (1991, Philippines), Eyjafjallajökull (2010, Iceland — disrupted air travel).

Tsunamis.

  • Caused by undersea earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, or landslides.
  • 2004 Indian Ocean Tsunami (Dec 26) — triggered by Mw 9.1 earthquake off Sumatra. ~ 230,000 dead across 14 countries. India: ~ 12,000+ dead; worst-hit Tamil Nadu (Nagapattinam, Karaikal, Cuddalore), Kerala, Andhra, Andaman & Nicobar.
  • After 2004, India set up the Indian Tsunami Early Warning System (ITEWS) at INCOIS, Hyderabad (operational since 2007).

Cyclones.

  • Names by region:
    • Hurricanes — Atlantic, eastern Pacific (USA, Caribbean).
    • Typhoons — northwest Pacific (Japan, Philippines, China).
    • Cyclones — Indian Ocean, southwest Pacific.
    • Willy-willy — Australia.
  • India: Bay of Bengal ~ 4x more cyclones than Arabian Sea.
  • Cyclone intensity scale (IMD): Cyclonic storm → Severe → Very Severe → Extremely Severe → Super Cyclonic Storm.
  • Recent severe cyclones: 1999 Odisha super cyclone, 2013 Phailin, 2014 Hudhud, 2018 Gaja (TN), 2019 Fani, 2020 Amphan, 2023 Biparjoy, 2023 Michaung (Chennai).
  • India's cyclone naming list (joint with 13 IO countries) — names like Amphan, Tej, Asna etc.

Floods.

  • India: ~ 12% of country flood-prone.
  • Most flood-prone states: Assam (Brahmaputra), Bihar (Kosi), West Bengal (Damodar, Hooghly), UP, Odisha, AP, Tamil Nadu (urban — Chennai 2015, 2023).
  • Mumbai floods (2005) — 944 mm rainfall in one day.
  • Chennai floods (Dec 2015 — extraordinary NE monsoon; over 500 mm in a few days).
  • Kerala 2018 floods — worst since 1924; entire state affected.
  • Causes: heavy rainfall, river overflow, dam failure, urban drainage, deforestation, climate change.

Drought.

  • India's drought-prone area: ~ 68% of total. Bundelkhand, Marathwada, Rayalaseema, interior Karnataka, north Gujarat are chronically drought-prone.
  • Three types: meteorological (low rainfall), hydrological (low water levels), agricultural (low soil moisture).
  • Recent severe droughts: 2002, 2009, 2015 (linked to El Niño).

Landslides.

  • Most common in: Himalayan states (Uttarakhand, HP, Sikkim), NE India, Nilgiris, parts of WG.
  • Triggers: heavy rainfall, earthquakes, deforestation, road cutting.
  • Famous: Kedarnath cloudburst & landslide (June 2013) — ~ 6,000 dead.
  • Wayanad landslide (Kerala, 2024) — over 400 dead.

Disaster Management in India.

Legal framework

  • Disaster Management Act, 2005 — institutional architecture.
  • National Disaster Management Authority (NDMA) — apex body; chaired by PM. HQ: New Delhi.
  • State Disaster Management Authority (SDMA) — chaired by CM.
  • District Disaster Management Authority (DDMA) — chaired by Collector.
  • National Institute of Disaster Management (NIDM) — training, research; New Delhi.
  • National Disaster Response Force (NDRF) — raised 2006; 16 battalions; first responder. HQ: New Delhi.
  • State Disaster Response Force (SDRF) — state versions.

Key plans & tools

  • National Disaster Management Plan — first in 2016; updated.
  • Sendai Framework (2015-2030) — global blueprint for DRR; replaces Hyogo Framework.
  • Coalition for Disaster Resilient Infrastructure (CDRI) — India-led international coalition, launched 2019.
  • Aapda Mitra — volunteer training scheme.
  • Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana — agricultural disaster insurance.
TAMIL NADU CONNECTION

Tamil Nadu has its own SDMA, SDRF, and disaster management plan. The 2004 tsunami was a turning point — TN coastal villages now have early warning sirens, evacuation routes, and concrete safe-houses. The TN State Disaster Management Authority was constituted in 2007.
