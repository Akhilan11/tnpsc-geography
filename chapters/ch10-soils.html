CHAPTER 10 / SOILS

Soils of India.

Thin, fragile, productive — the layer of weathered rock that decides what India eats.

OBJECTIVES OF THIS CHAPTER

  • Understand the factors that form soil and define key terms (regolith, horizons, pH).
  • Identify the eight ICAR soil types of India with their distribution and properties.
  • Match each soil type to the crops it supports.
  • Distinguish Khadar from Bhangar; Regur from Laterite.
  • Describe the causes of soil erosion and methods of conservation.

Soil is the topmost layer of the earth's crust — a mixture of weathered rock, decayed organic matter (humus), water, air, and living organisms. It is rarely more than a metre thick, yet without it almost no civilisation could feed itself. India's astonishing agricultural diversity — paddy in the Cauvery delta, wheat in the Punjab, cotton on the Deccan, coffee in the Western Ghats, tea on the Brahmaputra slopes — is finally explained by the diversity of its soils.

How soil forms.

Soil formation, or pedogenesis, is governed by five factors: parent rock, climate, relief, vegetation/organisms, and time. Weathering breaks rock into fine particles; organic matter accumulates; water moves substances up and down; the result is a vertically layered profile of horizons: O (organic litter), A (topsoil, dark, fertile), B (subsoil, where leached materials accumulate), C (weathered parent material), R (bedrock).

ICAR classification — eight types.

The Indian Council of Agricultural Research recognises eight major soil types:

  1. Alluvial soil
  2. Black (Regur) soil
  3. Red & yellow soil
  4. Laterite soil
  5. Arid / desert soil
  6. Saline & alkaline soil
  7. Peaty & marshy soil
  8. Forest & mountain soil

1. Alluvial soil — the breadbasket soil.

The most widespread soil in India, covering about 43% of land area (~ 14.3 lakh sq km). Deposited by rivers — primarily the Himalayan rivers in the Northern Plains, and by peninsular east-flowing rivers in coastal deltas. Composition: silt-sand-clay; rich in potash and lime, but deficient in nitrogen and humus.

Two sub-types

  • Bhangar — older alluvium, above flood level, contains kankar (calcareous nodules). Less fertile.
  • Khadar — newer alluvium, in active floodplains, refreshed each year. Highly fertile.

Crops: wheat, rice, sugarcane, oilseeds, pulses, jute, cotton.

2. Black soil (Regur).

Derived from the weathering of the Deccan Trap basalt. Black colour comes from titaniferous magnetite and humus. Found in Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, parts of Tamil Nadu (e.g. Coimbatore, Salem, Ramanathapuram). Covers about 15% of India.

Properties

  • Self-ploughing — develops deep cracks in summer that mix the layers, eliminating the need to plough.
  • Highly retentive of moisture — holds water like a sponge.
  • Rich in calcium carbonate, magnesium, potash, lime; poor in nitrogen, phosphorus, organic matter.
  • Sticky when wet, hard when dry.

Crops: cotton (hence "black cotton soil"), wheat, jowar, linseed, oilseeds, tobacco, sugarcane, citrus.

KEY FACT

"Regur" is from the Telugu word for cotton soil. Three Indian states — Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat — together account for nearly all of India's black soil area.

3. Red & yellow soil.

Develops on crystalline igneous rocks (granite, gneiss) under conditions of moderate rainfall. The red colour is from iron oxide (hematite); yellow shades occur where the iron is hydrated. Covers about 18% of India.

Distribution

Eastern peninsular India: Tamil Nadu (most of TN soils are red), Odisha, eastern AP, Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, parts of MP, Karnataka.

Properties

  • Poor in nitrogen, phosphorus, humus.
  • Porous, friable; needs fertiliser & irrigation.

Crops: millets (ragi, jowar), groundnut, potato, pulses, oilseeds. With irrigation: paddy, sugarcane.

4. Laterite soil.

Forms in tropical climates with seasonal alternation of wet and dry seasons. Heavy monsoon rains leach away silica, leaving behind iron and aluminium oxides — hence laterite is rich in iron and aluminium but poor in nitrogen, phosphorus, calcium. The name comes from Latin later = brick.

Distribution

Western Ghats summits (Karnataka, Kerala), Eastern Ghats (Odisha), parts of Assam, Meghalaya, West Bengal, Tamil Nadu (parts of Nilgiris, Salem).

Two varieties: high-level laterite (coarse, gravelly, on plateau summits) and low-level laterite (finer, on lower slopes, used for cultivation with fertilisers). Building blocks made from laterite are used for traditional Kerala architecture.

Crops: cashew, tea, coffee, rubber, coconut, tapioca. Important commercial-crop soil.

5. Arid / desert soil.

Found in Rajasthan (Thar), parts of Gujarat (Kachchh), southern Punjab and Haryana. Sandy texture (90% sand + 5% clay), low organic matter, high salt content, and a hard "kankar" layer below the surface that restricts water percolation. With irrigation, crops can be grown — but salinity is a recurring problem. Indira Gandhi Canal has converted parts of western Rajasthan into wheat country.

6. Forest & mountain soil.

Found in Himalayan slopes, Western Ghats summits, Eastern Ghats, NE India. Two sub-types:

  • Coniferous slopes (higher altitudes) — thin, acidic, podzolic.
  • Deciduous slopes (lower altitudes) — rich in humus, fertile.

Supports tea, coffee, spices, fruits, medicinal plants.

7. Saline & alkaline soil — Usar, Reh, Kallar, Chopan.

Forms in regions of poor drainage with high evaporation, where salts (sodium, magnesium, calcium) accumulate on the surface as a white crust ("Reh"). Found in Punjab, Haryana, western UP, Bihar, coastal Maharashtra, Sundarbans, Rann of Kachchh. Canal irrigation without drainage is a frequent cause (waterlogging → salt rise). Treated by leaching, gypsum, and growing salt-tolerant crops like dhaincha and barley.

8. Peaty & marshy soil.

Forms in waterlogged, anaerobic environments where dead vegetation accumulates faster than it decomposes. Found in Kerala backwaters (Kuttanad — below sea level paddy cultivation!), Sundarbans, Brahmaputra plain, the coast of Tamil Nadu (Adyar, Pichavaram mangroves). Highly acidic, very rich in organic matter.

Soil erosion & conservation.

Types of erosion

  • Sheet erosion — uniform removal of topsoil by rain.
  • Rill erosion — small channels formed by running water.
  • Gully erosion — deep ravines (Chambal badlands).
  • Wind erosion — dominant in Rajasthan, parts of Gujarat.
  • Glacial erosion — limited to high Himalayan zones.

Conservation measures

  • Contour ploughing & terracing on slopes.
  • Shelterbelts & afforestation against wind erosion.
  • Strip cropping & crop rotation.
  • Check dams in gullied land.
  • Mulching, green manuring.
TAMIL NADU CONNECTION

TN has all major soil types in some measure: red soil (largest share, ~ 65% of TN), black soil (Coimbatore, Salem, Ramanathapuram), alluvial (Cauvery delta), laterite (Nilgiris, parts of Salem), and coastal saline / peaty soils along the Coromandel.

QUICK RECAP

What this chapter said.

  • Eight ICAR soil types: Alluvial, Black (Regur), Red & Yellow, Laterite, Arid, Forest, Saline & Alkaline, Peaty & Marshy.
  • Alluvial: 43% of India's area; sub-types Khadar (new, fertile) and Bhangar (old).
  • Black (Regur): from Deccan Trap basalt; self-ploughing; cotton soil.
  • Red & Yellow: 18% of India; eastern peninsular India; needs fertiliser.
  • Laterite: tropical leached soil; cashew, tea, coffee, rubber zones.
  • Arid: Rajasthan; sandy, saline; kankar pan beneath.
  • Conservation: contour ploughing, terracing, shelterbelts, check dams.

Practice MCQs

CH 10 · 10 QUESTIONS
01The "self-ploughing" property is associated with which soil?
  1. Alluvial
  2. Red
  3. Black (Regur)
  4. Laterite
Reveal answer

C — Black (Regur). Deep cracks in summer mix the layers naturally.

02Laterite soil is best suited for —
  1. Wheat & mustard
  2. Paddy in flood plain
  3. Cashew, tea, coffee, rubber
  4. Cotton
Reveal answer

C — Cashew, tea, coffee, rubber. The commercial-crop soil.

03"Khadar" refers to —
  1. Older alluvium with kankar
  2. Newer alluvium, fertile
  3. Saline soil
  4. Black cotton soil
Reveal answer

B — Newer alluvium. Refreshed every monsoon, very fertile.

04The red colour of red soils is due to —
  1. Aluminium oxide
  2. Iron oxide (hematite)
  3. Manganese oxide
  4. Silica
Reveal answer

B — Iron oxide (hematite).

05Regur soil is the weathering product of —
  1. Granite
  2. Sandstone
  3. Basalt (Deccan Trap)
  4. Limestone
Reveal answer

C — Basalt (Deccan Trap).

06The Kuttanad region of Kerala, where paddy is grown below sea level, is famous for which soil?
  1. Laterite
  2. Black
  3. Peaty & marshy
  4. Red & yellow
Reveal answer

C — Peaty & marshy.

07Chambal "badlands" are an example of which kind of erosion?
  1. Sheet
  2. Rill
  3. Gully
  4. Wind
Reveal answer

C — Gully erosion, producing the famous ravines.

08Saline soils in north India are locally called —
  1. Bhabar
  2. Reh / Usar
  3. Khadar
  4. Bhangar
Reveal answer

B — Reh / Usar.

09Alluvial soils cover what fraction of India's land area approximately?
  1. 15%
  2. 28%
  3. 43%
  4. 60%
Reveal answer

C — about 43%.

10Most of Tamil Nadu is dominated by which soil type?
  1. Alluvial
  2. Red
  3. Black
  4. Laterite
Reveal answer

B — Red soil, ~ 65% of TN area.
