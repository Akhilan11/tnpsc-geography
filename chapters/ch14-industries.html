CHAPTER 14 / INDUSTRIES

Industries.

From the steel mills of Jamshedpur to the IT corridors of Bengaluru and Chennai — how India makes things.

OBJECTIVES

  • Identify factors influencing industrial location.
  • Map the major industries — iron-steel, textiles, sugar, cement, chemicals, automobile, IT.
  • Memorise plant locations and leading-producer states.
  • Locate India's six major industrial regions and the modern industrial corridors.
  • Recognise India's sunrise sectors — semiconductors, EVs, renewables, defence, aerospace.
  • Understand the role of MSMEs and Special Economic Zones (SEZs) in the industrial economy.

Industry is the branch of geography where the country's wealth is actually forged. India today is the world's fifth-largest economy by nominal GDP, with a manufacturing sector worth roughly ₹40 lakh crore (~ 17% of GDP). But this national aggregate hides an intensely uneven map: nearly two-thirds of India's factory output comes from just six industrial regions, and within each of those regions, most output is concentrated in three or four cities. Studying industrial geography is therefore the study of why the map is so lumpy — why steel clusters near Jharkhand's coal, why cotton mills stayed in Ahmedabad and Coimbatore long after their original founders were gone, and why the world's iPhones are now assembled in a village called Sriperumbudur.

Factors of industrial location.

Alfred Weber's classical theory (1909) argued that a factory locates where the total transport cost of raw materials in and finished goods out is minimised. Modern industrial geography adds many more variables, but the underlying idea survives: every industry has a characteristic pull towards a specific type of place.

  • Raw materials — heavy industries near sources.
  • Power — coal, electricity, gas.
  • Labour — skilled (IT, electronics) vs unskilled.
  • Transport — rail, road, port, pipeline.
  • Markets — perishable industries (food) near consumers.
  • Capital & finance — Mumbai, Delhi, Chennai, Bengaluru.
  • Government policy — SEZs, incentives, freight equalisation (historical).
  • Water — heavy water user industries (textile, chemicals, paper).
  • Agglomeration economies — the tendency of similar firms to cluster (auto-component makers around Chennai, IT firms around Bengaluru) so they can share suppliers, workers, and knowledge.
TNPSC TIP — WEIGHT-LOSING vs WEIGHT-GAINING

Industries whose raw materials lose weight during processing (iron ore → steel; sugarcane → sugar) locate near the raw material source. Industries whose products gain weight or bulk (bottled drinks, furniture, bread) locate near the market. This "weight-loss ratio" is a classic TNPSC one-liner: the higher it is, the more raw-material-oriented the industry.

Iron and steel industry.

Iron and steel is the "mother industry" — no other heavy sector can grow without it. India's steel industry was born in 1907 when Jamsetji Tata's TISCO commissioned its first blast furnace at Sakchi (renamed Jamshedpur), fifteen years after Jamsetji first envisioned the plant. Independent India then built five public-sector integrated steel plants between 1955 and 1965 as the physical backbone of Nehru's industrial policy — each with a different foreign collaborator, giving a Cold War map of who supported whom.

The most basic of heavy industries. India is the world's 2nd largest crude steel producer (after China). National capacity: ~ 161 MT (2024). Major integrated steel plants:

MNEMONIC — FIVE PSU STEEL PLANTS & COLLABORATORS

"Rourkela Germany, Bhilai + Bokaro Soviet, Durgapur UK, Vizag Indigenous." (RGBSDUK-VI). Rourkela was West Germany's contribution, Bhilai and Bokaro came from the USSR, Durgapur from Britain, and Visakhapatnam (RINL, 1990s) was the first fully indigenous integrated plant, coastal so it can import ore easily.

KEY FACT — INDIA'S STEEL POSITION

India crossed China as the world's top consumer of steel per rupee of GDP growth in the mid-2010s but overall production is still second: China produces ~ 1,000 MT/year, India ~ 140 MT. India's National Steel Policy 2017 targets 300 MT/year capacity by 2030-31. Per-capita consumption is only ~ 87 kg (world average ~ 230 kg) — headroom to grow is huge.

Cotton textile industry.

The Indian cotton textile industry is the country's oldest organised industry and its second-largest employer after agriculture — approximately 4.5 crore direct workers. Cowasjee Nanabhoy Davar's mill opened in Bombay in 1854, and by 1900 there were 178 mills across the country. The industry's map was drawn by three simple facts: cotton grew on the black soil of the Deccan, humid coasts kept the yarn from breaking, and colonial ports controlled the export trade. Every classical textile centre — Bombay, Ahmedabad, Sholapur, Coimbatore — is a black-soil-plus-humid-air-plus-port intersection.

Two structural halves

  • Organised (mill) sector — large integrated mills; declining relative share.
  • Decentralised (handloom + powerloom) sector — powerlooms produce ~ 60% of India's cloth; handlooms remain the second-largest source of rural employment after agriculture.

TAMIL NADU CONNECTION — TIRUPPUR

The Tiruppur cluster produces cotton knitwear worth over ₹35,000 crore in exports (2023-24), sending T-shirts to Walmart, H&M, Zara, and Primark. What began in the 1960s as a small hosiery cluster is now the world's second-largest knitwear export hub. Water is the constraint: Tiruppur's dyeing units caused the pollution of the Noyyal River that ended in the 2011 Supreme Court order enforcing zero liquid discharge — a first for any Indian industrial cluster.

The first modern industry in India (Bombay 1854 — Cowasjee Davar). India is the world's 2nd largest producer of cotton textiles. Major centres: Maharashtra (Mumbai, Pune, Sholapur, Nagpur), Gujarat (Ahmedabad — "Manchester of India", Surat, Vadodara), Tamil Nadu (Coimbatore — "Manchester of South India", Tiruppur — knitwear capital, Madurai, Salem, Karur), West Bengal (Kolkata, Howrah), Punjab (Ludhiana — hosiery).

Jute industry.

Jute — the "golden fibre" — is India's second natural-fibre industry after cotton, and its most geographically concentrated. Almost every one of the 93 operating jute mills lies within a 90-kilometre strip of the Hugli river north and south of Kolkata: Titagarh, Hukumchand, Kankinara, Bhadreshwar, Rishra, Chengail. The concentration is not accidental — jute needs clean, soft river water for retting (soaking the raw stems), high humidity to keep fibres flexible, cheap coal from the Ranigunj-Jharia belt, and a port for export.

Partition in 1947 was catastrophic: the fibre-growing districts of eastern Bengal went to East Pakistan (now Bangladesh) while the mills stayed in India, forcing India to plant jute in the Terai and to import raw jute for years. Today, India is again the world's largest producer of raw jute and jute goods (about 55% of world output), with West Bengal producing over 75% of Indian jute. The industry is protected by the Jute Packaging Materials Act 1987, which mandates jute packaging for food grains and sugar. Modern challenges: competition from synthetic sacks, ageing mills, low labour productivity.

Sugar industry.

Sugar is India's second-largest agro-based industry after textiles — 732 operating mills, ~ 5 lakh workers, and over 5 crore cane-growing farmers depend on it. India alternates with Brazil for the world's top spot in sugar production, and holds first place in sugar consumption.

The sugar map has been quietly shifting for two decades. Uttar Pradesh and Bihar dominated the industry in the 1960s (colonial belt on the Ganga plain), but Maharashtra, Karnataka, and Tamil Nadu have overtaken them for one straightforward reason: sucrose content is 2-3 percentage points higher in tropical cane, the crushing season is longer (5-6 months vs 3-4), and cooperative sugar mills in Maharashtra have been more efficient than the private UP mills. Today Maharashtra typically produces the largest quantity of sugar in India, while UP still has the largest area under cane.

KEY FACT — WHY SUGAR MILLS ARE NEAR CANE FIELDS

Sugarcane loses ~ 0.5% of its sucrose per day after harvest, and the plant is heavy (weight-losing raw material). Every extra kilometre from field to mill therefore cuts recovery. The 24-hour "field-to-mill" rule effectively caps a sugar mill's economic catchment at ~ 25 km. This is why sugar mills scatter through the countryside instead of clustering in cities.

Cement industry.

India is the 2nd largest cement producer in the world after China (~ 8% of global production; installed capacity ~ 600 MT/year in 2024). Cement is a classic raw-material-oriented industry — you need roughly 1.5 tonnes of limestone plus 0.3 tonnes of coal to make 1 tonne of cement — so every cement plant sits on a limestone deposit, with a coal or gas source within reach.

Leading producer states

  • Rajasthan — historically the largest producer; plants at Chittaurgarh, Sirohi, Nimbahera, Beawar. Limestone belt around the Aravallis.
  • Andhra Pradesh & Telangana — Vijayawada region; Cuddapah limestone; Nalgonda district houses the Deccan's largest cluster.
  • Tamil Nadu — Ariyalur (the cement capital of TN), Dalmiapuram, Sankaridurg, Ramanathapuram.
  • Madhya Pradesh — Katni, Satna, Neemuch.
  • Karnataka, Gujarat, Chhattisgarh — major producers with limestone from Bagalkot, Saurashtra, and Raipur respectively.

India's first cement plant was set up at Porbandar, Gujarat, in 1904. The industry took off after independence with the Cement Corporation of India, but was fully de-licensed only in 1991. Today the industry is highly consolidated — the top four groups (UltraTech, Ambuja/ACC, Shree, Dalmia) control over 60% of capacity.

TAMIL NADU CONNECTION — ARIYALUR

Ariyalur district is Tamil Nadu's cement capital, producing over 40% of the state's output. The district sits on Cretaceous-age fossiliferous limestone (the same beds contain famous dinosaur fossils excavated near Kallakkurichi). Ariyalur cluster has plants of Dalmia, Ramco, Chettinad, India Cements, and Madras Cements — the reason a small district is one of India's most industrialised.

Chemicals, fertilizers, petrochemicals.

Fertilizers

India is the 3rd largest fertilizer producer in the world. Key plants: Sindri (Jharkhand, India's first 1951), Trombay, Namrup (Assam — natural gas), Vadodara, Kanpur, Nangal, Coromandel (Visakhapatnam), Mangalore.

Petrochemicals

Concentrated at: Reliance Jamnagar; IOC Panipat; Mangalore Chemicals & Fertilizers; Vadodara IPCL; Manali (Chennai).

Chemicals

Vadodara, Mumbai, Ahmedabad, Vapi (Gujarat), Cuddalore, Manali (TN), Hyderabad (Pharma — bulk drugs).

Automobile industry.

India is the 3rd largest automobile market in the world (passenger + commercial). Hubs:

  • Chennai — "Detroit of India" — Hyundai, Ford (former), Renault-Nissan, BMW, Daimler, Royal Enfield (Tiruvottiyur), Ashok Leyland, TVS, Hindustan Motors. Largest auto exporter in India.
  • Delhi NCR (Gurugram, Manesar, Noida) — Maruti Suzuki, Honda, Hero MotoCorp, Yamaha.
  • Pune-Aurangabad-Nashik (Maharashtra) — Tata Motors, Mahindra, Bajaj, Fiat, Mercedes-Benz, Volkswagen.
  • Hosur (TN) & Bengaluru (Karnataka) — Toyota Kirloskar, TVS, Ola Electric.
  • Sanand (Gujarat) — Tata Nano (former), Ford, Maruti.
  • Jamshedpur — Tata Motors trucks.
TAMIL NADU CONNECTION — DETROIT OF INDIA

Tamil Nadu has the largest concentration of auto and auto-component industries in India. Chennai's Ennore-Tiruvallur-Sriperumbudur belt, combined with Hosur, produces nearly 30% of India's cars and 35% of its commercial vehicles. Major manufacturers: Hyundai (largest), Renault-Nissan, BMW, Daimler/Bharat Benz, Ashok Leyland, TVS, Royal Enfield, Ford (factory now Tata's).

IT & electronics industry.

India is the world's largest IT-BPM exporter. Total IT sector revenue (2024): ~ $250 billion, of which ~ $200 billion is exports. The biggest hubs:

  • Bengaluru — "Silicon Valley of India" — Karnataka.
  • Hyderabad — "Cyberabad" — Telangana.
  • Chennai & Tamil Nadu (Coimbatore, Tiruchi) — major IT corridor (OMR/Old Mahabalipuram Road).
  • Gurugram, Noida, Delhi NCR.
  • Pune (Maharashtra).
  • Kolkata, Bhubaneswar, Kochi/Thiruvananthapuram, Mumbai.

Major Indian IT companies: TCS (Tata, headquartered Mumbai), Infosys (Bengaluru), Wipro (Bengaluru), HCL (Noida), Tech Mahindra (Pune).

Electronics manufacturing

India is the world's 2nd largest mobile phone manufacturer after China — about 300 million units in 2023-24, of which nearly a fifth was exported. The Production Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme launched in 2020 is the main policy lever: firms get 4-6% cashback on incremental sales, and it has already pulled Apple's iPhone assembly (Foxconn, Pegatron, Wistron/Tata) to Tamil Nadu and Karnataka. Major hubs:

  • Sriperumbudur & Kancheepuram (TN) — Foxconn (iPhones for Apple), Pegatron, Salcomp (chargers), Nokia Solutions.
  • Noida (UP) — Samsung's world's-largest phone factory; Oppo, Vivo, Xiaomi contract manufacturers.
  • Bengaluru & Kolar (Karnataka) — Foxconn (iPhone Pro assembly, new plant 2024); ISRO ecosystem.
  • Hyderabad — Foxconn, Micron (India's first semiconductor plant, approved 2023).

Sunrise sectors — where the next decade is being built.

Semiconductors

The India Semiconductor Mission (ISM, 2021) committed ₹76,000 crore of incentives. First approved projects: Micron's assembly-and-test plant at Sanand (Gujarat, 2023) — India's first commercial semiconductor plant; Tata Electronics fab at Dholera (Gujarat, 28nm) and OSAT at Jagiroad (Assam); Kaynes-Renesas OSAT at Sanand. Tamil Nadu's Semiconductor Policy 2024 targets Kancheepuram-Sriperumbudur belt.

Electric vehicles & batteries

India is the world's 3rd largest 2-wheeler EV market. Ola Electric (Krishnagiri, TN — world's largest 2W EV factory), Ather, TVS (Hosur), Bajaj (Pune) dominate. Passenger EVs: Tata Motors (Sanand, Pune), Mahindra (Chakan), Hyundai (Chennai). The FAME-II and PLI-Automobile / PLI-ACC schemes drive investment.

Renewable energy manufacturing

India's target of 500 GW non-fossil capacity by 2030 has spawned solar-module giants (Adani, Waaree, Tata Power Solar in Bengaluru), battery-cell factories (Reliance, ACC in Hosur), and green-hydrogen plans (Reliance Jamnagar, NTPC Andhra).

Defence & aerospace

Defence exports grew from ₹1,940 crore (2014-15) to over ₹21,000 crore (2023-24). Hubs: HAL Bengaluru (aircraft), HAL Kanpur (Dornier), BEL Bengaluru, DRDO clusters at Hyderabad, Balasore, Chennai. India's Defence Industrial Corridors are being built at UP (Aligarh-Kanpur-Lucknow-Jhansi-Chitrakoot) and TN (Chennai-Hosur-Salem-Coimbatore-Trichy).

Six major industrial regions of India.

  1. Mumbai-Pune region — Maharashtra. Cotton textiles, chemicals, petrochem, autos, films.
  2. Hugli region — West Bengal. Jute, paper, engineering, chemicals.
  3. Bengaluru-Chennai region — Karnataka & Tamil Nadu. Textiles, autos, electronics, IT.
  4. Gujarat region — Ahmedabad-Vadodara-Surat-Jamnagar. Textiles, chemicals, petrochem, diamonds.
  5. Chotanagpur region — Jharkhand, WB, Odisha, Chhattisgarh. Iron & steel, heavy engineering.
  6. Delhi NCR region — Delhi, Gurugram, Noida, Faridabad, Ghaziabad. Autos, electronics, ready-made garments.

Industrial corridors (government initiative)

  • Delhi-Mumbai Industrial Corridor (DMIC) — ~ 1,504 km along the Western Dedicated Freight Corridor; 8 nodes across 6 states; Japanese collaboration.
  • Chennai-Bengaluru Industrial Corridor (CBIC) — 3 nodes: Ponneri (TN), Krishnapatnam (AP), Tumakuru (Karnataka). Japanese-supported.
  • Amritsar-Kolkata Industrial Corridor (AKIC) — ~ 1,839 km along the Eastern Dedicated Freight Corridor; 20 nodes across 7 states.
  • Vizag-Chennai Industrial Corridor (VCIC) — ADB-supported; part of East Coast Economic Corridor.
  • Bengaluru-Mumbai Economic Corridor (BMEC) — UK collaboration.
  • East Coast Economic Corridor (ECEC) — India's first coastal corridor, Kolkata to Kanyakumari.

MSMEs and Special Economic Zones.

Micro, Small & Medium Enterprises

MSMEs are the backbone of Indian manufacturing that never make headlines. There are approximately 6.3 crore MSMEs in India, contributing ~ 30% of GDP, 40% of exports, and employing over 11 crore people — second only to agriculture. The MSMED Act 2006 classifies enterprises by investment + turnover (revised 2020):

  • Micro: investment < ₹1 crore, turnover < ₹5 crore.
  • Small: investment < ₹10 crore, turnover < ₹50 crore.
  • Medium: investment < ₹50 crore, turnover < ₹250 crore.

Major MSME clusters: Ludhiana (bicycles, hosiery), Tiruppur (knitwear), Sivakasi (matches, fireworks, printing), Kanpur (leather), Firozabad (glass bangles), Moradabad (brassware), Jodhpur (handicrafts).

Special Economic Zones (SEZs)

SEZs are duty-free enclaves treated as foreign territory for trade and tariff purposes. Introduced through the SEZ Act 2005. India has ~ 280 operational SEZs, mostly IT/ITES parks. The concept was piloted at Kandla, Gujarat (1965) — Asia's first EPZ (Export Processing Zone). Tamil Nadu has the largest number of operating SEZs, especially in IT (Chennai's OMR, Sriperumbudur, Coimbatore).

TAMIL NADU CONNECTION — SIVAKASI

Sivakasi in Virudhunagar district is a unique triple-industry MSME town: it produces about 90% of India's fireworks, 80% of the safety matches, and hosts one of the country's largest offset-printing clusters (calendars, textbooks, labels). All three industries co-exist because the dry climate suits chemicals, the labour force is skilled, and the printing units use the same tightly-linked supply chains. Around 8 lakh people depend on Sivakasi's cottage economy.
