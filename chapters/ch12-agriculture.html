CHAPTER 12 / AGRICULTURE

Agriculture in India.

Agriculture employs nearly half of India's workforce, produces ~ 17% of GDP, and feeds 1.4 billion people. It is the most important sector in the syllabus.

OBJECTIVES

  • Identify the major types of farming practised in India.
  • Distinguish kharif, rabi, and zaid cropping seasons.
  • Recall the leading producer states of every major crop.
  • Know the four "colour revolutions" of Indian agriculture.
  • Understand the key policy instruments: MSP, FCI, APMC, PDS.

Agriculture is the backbone of the Indian economy. As of 2024, agriculture and allied sectors contribute about 17–18% of GDP but employ approximately 45–46% of the workforce. India is the world's largest producer of milk, pulses, jute, spices; the second-largest producer of rice, wheat, cotton, sugarcane, vegetables, fruits, groundnut. Despite a shrinking GDP share, agriculture remains politically and socially central.

Types of farming.

Subsistence farming

  • Cultivation for the family's own use.
  • Small holdings, primitive tools, low output.
  • Practised throughout rural India historically.

Commercial farming

  • For the market, with high inputs.
  • Punjab, Haryana, parts of UP, AP, TN.
  • Capital-intensive; uses HYV seeds, fertilizers, machinery.

Plantation farming

  • Single crop over a large estate.
  • Tea (Assam, WB, Nilgiris); coffee (Karnataka, Kerala, TN); rubber (Kerala); spices (Kerala, TN).

Shifting (jhum) cultivation

  • Slash-and-burn; abandon plots after a few seasons.
  • NE India (Mizoram, Nagaland, Manipur, Tripura).
  • Various local names: Bewar, Penda, Kumri, Podu, Pamlou.

Intensive farming

  • Maximum output per unit area.
  • High labour or capital input.
  • Kerala, West Bengal, parts of TN.

Dry-land & wet-land

  • Dry-land (< 75 cm rain): millets, gram, mustard.
  • Wet-land (> 75 cm): rice, jute, sugarcane.

The three cropping seasons.

SeasonMonthsMajor Crops
Kharif (monsoon)June–Oct (sown with SW monsoon, harvested in autumn)Rice, maize, jowar, bajra, ragi, tur (arhar), moong, urad, cotton, jute, groundnut, soybean, sugarcane
Rabi (winter)Oct–March (sown in winter, harvested in spring)Wheat, barley, gram (chana), peas, mustard, linseed
Zaid (summer)March–June (between rabi and kharif)Watermelon, cucumber, muskmelon, fodder crops, vegetables. Mostly under irrigation.
TAMIL NADU CONNECTION — TN CROPPING SEASONS

Tamil Nadu has its own season names tied to the NE monsoon: Sornavari (April–July, kharif-like) — sown with the SW monsoon; Samba (August–January, main rice season) — sown with the NE monsoon; Navarai/Thaladi (January–April, rabi-like). Samba is the dominant paddy season in TN.

Food grains — the staples.

Rice / Paddy

  • India is the 2nd largest producer in world (after China), accounting for ~ 24% of global rice.
  • Requires hot wet climate; > 100 cm rainfall or irrigation.
  • Leading states (in order of production): West Bengal, Uttar Pradesh, Punjab, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Odisha, Tamil Nadu, Bihar, Chhattisgarh.
  • Crop seasons: kharif (largest), rabi (in southern states), summer.
  • India is the largest exporter of rice in the world.

Wheat

  • 2nd largest producer (after China). Indian wheat ~ 13% of world production.
  • Cool dry winter; bright sunshine at ripening. 50–75 cm rainfall.
  • Leading states: Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Punjab, Haryana, Rajasthan.
  • "Rice-Wheat" belt of Punjab-Haryana-W UP is the heart of the Green Revolution.

Millets — coarse cereals

  • Jowar (sorghum) — drought-resistant; Maharashtra, Karnataka, MP, TN.
  • Bajra (pearl millet) — sandy soils; Rajasthan (largest), UP, Haryana, Gujarat, MH.
  • Ragi (finger millet) — red soils; Karnataka (largest), Odisha, AP, TN, Maharashtra.
  • 2018 declared "Year of Millets" in India; UN declared 2023 "International Year of Millets" on India's proposal.

Pulses

  • India is the world's largest producer and consumer of pulses (~ 25% of global output).
  • Major pulses: gram, tur (arhar), urad, moong, masoor.
  • Leading states: Madhya Pradesh (largest), Rajasthan, Maharashtra, UP, Karnataka.

Cash crops.

Sugarcane

India is the 2nd largest producer (after Brazil). Leading states: Uttar Pradesh (largest), Maharashtra, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Bihar. UP produces most by volume, Maharashtra leads in productivity. Sugarcane is grown in both north and south but yields are much higher in the south due to better climate.

Cotton

India is the largest producer of cotton in the world. Needs 200 frost-free days. Three main belts:

  • Northern (semi-arid) — Punjab, Haryana, Rajasthan, north MP. Irrigated; American varieties.
  • Central (black soil) — Maharashtra, Gujarat, MP. Largest. Rain-fed.
  • Southern — Telangana, AP, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu. Mostly irrigated.

Top three states: Gujarat, Maharashtra, Telangana.

Jute

India is the world's largest producer of jute (~ 50% of global). Requires hot humid climate & alluvial soil. Almost all in: West Bengal (75%+), Assam, Bihar, Odisha.

Oilseeds

  • Groundnut — Gujarat (largest), Andhra Pradesh, TN, Karnataka, Rajasthan.
  • Mustard / Rapeseed — Rajasthan, MP, Haryana, UP, WB.
  • Soybean — Madhya Pradesh (largest), Maharashtra, Rajasthan.
  • Sunflower — Karnataka, AP, Maharashtra.

Plantation crops.

Tea

India is the 2nd largest producer of tea (after China) and 4th largest exporter. Needs hot climate (20–30°C), heavy rainfall (150–250 cm), well-drained sloping ground. Assam (largest, ~ 50%), West Bengal (Darjeeling, Dooars), Tamil Nadu (Nilgiris, Anaimalai), Kerala.

Coffee

India produces ~ 4% of world coffee. Two types: Arabica (premium, higher altitudes) and Robusta (sturdier, more produced). Almost all of India's coffee comes from the Western Ghats: Karnataka (largest, ~ 70%), Kerala, Tamil Nadu.

Rubber

India is among the top 5 producers. Almost exclusively grown in Kerala (~ 90%), Tamil Nadu (Kanyakumari, Nilgiris), Karnataka, Tripura, Assam.

Spices

India is the world's largest producer, consumer and exporter of spices. Major spices and their leading state:

  • Pepper, cardamom (small) — Kerala
  • Cardamom (large), turmeric, ginger — Sikkim leads in large cardamom; turmeric: Telangana & Tamil Nadu (Erode); ginger: Karnataka, Meghalaya.
  • Chillies — Andhra Pradesh (Guntur).
  • Cumin, coriander, fennel, fenugreek — Rajasthan, Gujarat.
  • Cloves, nutmeg, mace — Kerala, Tamil Nadu.

Horticulture & allied sectors.

India is the 2nd largest producer of fruits and vegetables in the world.

  • Banana: Tamil Nadu (largest), Maharashtra, AP, Gujarat. India is largest banana producer in world.
  • Mango: UP, AP, Karnataka, Bihar, TN. India produces ~ 40% of world's mangoes.
  • Coconut: Kerala, TN, Karnataka, AP. India is among top 3 producers.
  • Cashew: Maharashtra, AP, Odisha, Karnataka, Kerala, TN.
  • Potato: UP, WB, Bihar, Gujarat. India 2nd largest world producer.
  • Onion: Maharashtra (Nashik), MP, Karnataka, Gujarat. India 2nd largest.
  • Tomato: AP, MP, Karnataka, Gujarat, Odisha.

Animal Husbandry & Dairy: India is world's largest milk producer (~ 24% of world output). UP, Rajasthan, MP, Gujarat lead. The "Anand pattern" (Amul) cooperative model is famous globally.

Fisheries: India is 2nd largest fish-producing nation; largest inland fish producer; major marine fishing on Konkan, Malabar, Coromandel coasts.

Policy & institutions.

  • Minimum Support Price (MSP) — recommended by the Commission for Agricultural Costs & Prices (CACP); covers 22 crops including 7 cereals, 5 pulses, 7 oilseeds, plus cotton, jute, sugarcane (FRP), copra.
  • Food Corporation of India (FCI) — set up 1965; procurement, storage, distribution.
  • APMC Act & Mandi system — regulated agricultural markets at the state level.
  • Public Distribution System (PDS) — National Food Security Act 2013 covers ~ 67% of population.
  • PM-KISAN (2019) — ₹6,000/year direct income support to farmers.
  • PMFBY (2016) — Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana, crop insurance.
  • Soil Health Cards (2015) — soil testing.
  • eNAM (2016) — electronic National Agricultural Market.
  • Krishi Vigyan Kendras (KVKs) — district agricultural extension centres.
