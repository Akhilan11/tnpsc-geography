CHAPTER 03 / PHYSIOGRAPHY · THE HIMALAYAS

The Himalayas.

The youngest, highest, and most consequential mountain system on Earth — and the wall that makes India climatically possible.

OBJECTIVES OF THIS CHAPTER

  • Explain the tectonic origin of the Himalayas and the role of the Tethys Sea.
  • Identify the three parallel ranges — Himadri, Himachal, Shivalik — and their distinguishing features.
  • Map the four regional divisions of the Himalayas from west to east.
  • Recall the major peaks, passes, and glaciers — with heights and locations.
  • Distinguish the Himalayas from the Trans-Himalayan ranges (Karakoram, Ladakh, Zanskar).
  • Describe the Purvanchal (Eastern Hills) and their distinct character.

The Himalayas are not just a row of mountains. They are the youngest of the world's great ranges, they hold the largest store of ice outside the poles, they feed the great rivers of South Asia, and — most crucially for us — they are the reason the Indian subcontinent has a monsoon. Without the Himalayan wall the cold air of Central Asia would sweep south and the warm moisture-laden winds of the Indian Ocean would have nothing to lift them. Strip the Himalayas off the map and the green India we know would be a desert. Every other chapter of this book — climate, rivers, soils, agriculture — leans on the chapter you are about to read.

Origin and tectonics.

The Himalayas were born of a slow, immense collision. Until about 180 million years ago, the Indian plate sat fused to Antarctica in the supercontinent of Gondwana, separated from Asia by a wide ocean called the Tethys Sea. As Gondwana broke up, the Indian plate drifted northward at the geologically rapid rate of about 15 cm per year, closing the Tethys.

About 55–50 million years ago, in the early Eocene, the Indian plate crashed into the Eurasian plate. The marine sediments lying on the floor of the Tethys had nowhere to go — they crumpled upward into a series of folds. Three great uplift events, separated by tens of millions of years, produced the three parallel ranges we see today. The collision continues; the Himalayas are still rising at 5 mm to 1 cm per year, which is why earthquakes are frequent across the entire arc.

KEY FACT — A YOUNG MOUNTAIN

The Himalayas are classified as fold mountains of the Tertiary period. They are among the youngest mountain ranges on Earth (compare with the Aravallis at ~2,500 million years, among the oldest). Marine fossils — ammonites, called Saligrams in Hindu tradition — are found in Himalayan rocks, proof that this land was once a sea floor.

The three parallel ranges.

From north to south, the Himalayas comprise three roughly parallel ranges. Each one is older than the one to its south.

1. The Greater Himalayas — Himadri

The northernmost, highest, and most continuous range. Average elevation: over 6,000 metres. Average width: 25 km. Covered in perennial snow and glaciers. The range houses all the world's highest peaks — Everest, Kanchenjunga, Nanda Devi, Annapurna. The folds in this range are asymmetrical, with a steep southern slope and a gentler northern slope. The core is composed of granites and crystalline rocks.

2. The Lesser Himalayas — Himachal

South of the Himadri. Elevation: 3,700 to 4,500 metres. Width: 60–80 km. Mostly composed of highly compressed and altered rocks. This is the range of the great Indian hill stations — Shimla, Mussoorie, Nainital, Ranikhet, Darjeeling, Chail. Sub-ranges include:

  • Pir Panjal — Kashmir region, the longest sub-range of the Himachal.
  • Dhauladhar — Himachal Pradesh; Dharamsala is on its slope.
  • Mussoorie / Nag Tibba — Uttarakhand.
  • Mahabharat range — Nepal sector.

3. The Outer Himalayas — Shivalik

The southernmost range. Elevation: 900 to 1,100 metres. Width: 10–50 km. Composed of unconsolidated sediments — gravel, sand, alluvium — washed down from the Himalayas. The youngest of the three ranges. Between the Shivalik and the Lesser Himalayas lie long, flat-bottomed valleys called Duns (in the west — Dehradun, Kotli Dun, Patli Dun) and Duars (in the east, in Assam–West Bengal).

MNEMONIC — THREE RANGES NORTH TO SOUTH

"H–H–S" or "Hi-mad–ri / Hi-ma–chal / Shi-va–lik" — easy because they are alphabetised by their initials only loosely; remember north→south is high→low.

Regional divisions — west to east.

From the Indus in the west to the Brahmaputra in the east, the Himalayan arc is conventionally divided into four sections.

1. Punjab Himalayas 560 km

  • Between the rivers Indus and Sutlej.
  • Spans Ladakh, J&K (UT), and Himachal Pradesh.
  • Major ranges: Karakoram (Trans), Ladakh (Trans), Zanskar, Pir Panjal, Dhauladhar.
  • Important features: Vale of Kashmir, Dal Lake.

2. Kumaon Himalayas 320 km

  • Between the Sutlej and the Kali (Sharda) rivers.
  • Lies entirely within Uttarakhand.
  • Peaks: Nanda Devi (7,816 m), Kamet, Trishul.
  • Hindu pilgrimage centres: Kedarnath, Badrinath, Gangotri, Yamunotri.

3. Nepal Himalayas 800 km

  • Between the Kali and the Tista rivers.
  • The tallest section — eight of the world's ten highest peaks.
  • Everest (8,848.86 m), Kanchenjunga, Lhotse, Makalu, Annapurna, Manaslu, Dhaulagiri.

4. Assam Himalayas 750 km

  • Between the Tista and the Brahmaputra (Dihang).
  • Covers Sikkim, Bhutan, and Arunachal Pradesh.
  • Lower elevations than Nepal, but receives the heaviest rainfall.
  • Important peaks: Namcha Barwa (in Tibet, where Brahmaputra makes its great bend).
MNEMONIC — REGIONAL DIVISIONS (W → E)

"P–K–N–A" — Punjab → Kumaon → Nepal → Assam. Or: "Please Keep Notes Always".

TNPSC TIP — "HIGHEST IN INDIA"

Three different "highest" facts often confuse:
• K2 (8,611 m) — highest in India if Pakistan-administered Kashmir is included (Indian govt. claims).
• Kanchenjunga (8,586 m) — highest peak entirely within India's effective control (Sikkim).
• Nanda Devi (7,816 m) — highest peak entirely within an Indian state (Uttarakhand), not a UT.

The Trans-Himalayas.

North of the Greater Himalayas lie a set of ranges that are technically not part of the Himalayas — they predate the great collision. Together they are called the Trans-Himalayas and they cover most of Ladakh.

  • Karakoram — the highest, includes K2 (8,611 m), Gasherbrum, Saser Kangri. The Siachen glacier is here. Russian and Indian armies have faced off at the Siachen since 1984.
  • Ladakh Range — runs through Ladakh; the Indus flows in a deep gorge between the Ladakh range and the Zanskar range.
  • Zanskar Range — south of the Ladakh range, north of the Greater Himalayas. The Zanskar river flows along it.
  • Kailash Range — in Tibet, north of the Indian Himalayas. Mount Kailash (6,638 m), sacred to four religions, is here. Mansarovar Lake lies at its base.

The Eastern Hills — Purvanchal.

At the easternmost end, near the bend of the Brahmaputra, the Himalayan ranges turn sharply southward. These southward-running hills, mostly covered with dense forest, are collectively called the Purvanchal or Eastern Hills. They are lower than the main Himalayas and form India's frontier with Myanmar.

  • Patkai Bum — northernmost; India–Myanmar border in Arunachal.
  • Naga Hills — Nagaland. Saramati (3,826 m) is the highest peak.
  • Manipur Hills — surround the Manipur valley; Loktak Lake is here (only floating national park, Keibul Lamjao).
  • Mizo Hills (Lushai Hills) — Mizoram. Highest: Blue Mountain (Phawngpui, 2,157 m).
  • Tripura Hills — extension into Tripura.
  • Barail Range — running through Assam, Nagaland, Manipur; separates the Brahmaputra valley from the Barak (Surma) valley.
KEY FACT — MEGHALAYA PLATEAU

The Meghalaya Plateau — Garo, Khasi, Jaintia Hills — is not part of the Himalayas. It is geologically a detached fragment of the Peninsular Plateau (we'll meet it in Chapter 5). Mawsynram and Cherrapunji on the Khasi Hills receive the world's highest annual rainfall (over 11,000 mm).

Significance of the Himalayas.

1. Climatic guardian

The Himalayas block the cold, dry winds of Central Asia from sweeping south into India. They also intercept the moisture-laden south-west monsoon winds, forcing them to release rain over the subcontinent. Without them, north India would be a temperate cold desert.

2. Source of perennial rivers

Twelve major rivers — Indus, Sutlej, Beas, Ravi, Chenab, Jhelum, Ganga, Yamuna, Ghaghara, Gandak, Kosi, Brahmaputra — and dozens of tributaries are fed by Himalayan glaciers and snowmelt, making them perennial. The entire Northern Plain is alluvium washed down by these rivers.

3. Fertile soils & agriculture

The sediment carried by Himalayan rivers built and continues to renew the Indo-Gangetic Plain — among the most agriculturally productive regions on Earth.

4. Defence and strategy

For most of history, the Himalayas formed an almost impassable northern frontier. Even today, the LAC and the high passes — Nathu La, Lipulekh, Karakoram Pass — are strategically critical.

5. Hydroelectric and forest resources

Fast-flowing rivers in deep gorges make the Himalayas the best zone for hydroelectric generation. Dense forests of pine, deodar, fir, oak provide timber.

6. Tourism, pilgrimage, biodiversity

Hill stations, Hindu pilgrimage centres (Char Dham), Buddhist monasteries, Sikh shrines (Hemkund Sahib), national parks (Great Himalayan, Jim Corbett, Khangchendzonga, Namdapha). The Himalayas are also one of the world's biodiversity hotspots.

TAMIL NADU CONNECTION

The Himalayas don't touch Tamil Nadu, but the shadow of the Himalayas does — the southwest monsoon weakened by the time it reaches the TN coast is the reason the state depends overwhelmingly on the northeast (retreating) monsoon for its rainfall, unlike most of India. We will return to this in Chapter 9.

Practice MCQs

CH 03 · 10 QUESTIONS
01The southernmost range of the Himalayas, composed mostly of unconsolidated sediments, is the —
  1. Himadri
  2. Himachal
  3. Shivalik
  4. Karakoram
Reveal answer

C — Shivalik (Outer Himalayas), 900–1,100 m, the youngest of the three ranges.

02The Pir Panjal range belongs to which division of the Himalayas?
  1. Himadri
  2. Himachal
  3. Shivalik
  4. Trans-Himalaya
Reveal answer

B — Himachal (Lesser Himalayas). Pir Panjal is the longest sub-range of the Lesser Himalayas; it borders the Vale of Kashmir on the south.

03The highest peak entirely within an Indian state (not a UT) is —
  1. K2
  2. Kanchenjunga
  3. Nanda Devi
  4. Trishul
Reveal answer

C — Nanda Devi (7,816 m), in Uttarakhand. Kanchenjunga lies on the Sikkim–Nepal border; K2 is in POK; Ladakh is a UT.

04The Gangotri glacier feeds which river headstream?
  1. Alaknanda
  2. Mandakini
  3. Bhagirathi
  4. Pindar
Reveal answer

C — Bhagirathi. The Bhagirathi joins the Alaknanda at Devprayag to form the Ganga.

05The Nepal Himalayas lie between which two rivers?
  1. Indus and Sutlej
  2. Sutlej and Kali
  3. Kali and Tista
  4. Tista and Brahmaputra
Reveal answer

C — Kali and Tista. West-to-east order: Punjab Himalayas (Indus–Sutlej), Kumaon (Sutlej–Kali), Nepal (Kali–Tista), Assam (Tista–Brahmaputra).

06The Karakoram, Ladakh, and Zanskar ranges are collectively called the —
  1. Himadri
  2. Trans-Himalayas
  3. Purvanchal
  4. Shivaliks
Reveal answer

B — Trans-Himalayas. They lie north of the Greater Himalayas and predate the great collision.

07The pass connecting Sikkim with Tibet, reopened for trade in 2006, is —
  1. Lipulekh
  2. Shipki La
  3. Nathu La
  4. Rohtang
Reveal answer

C — Nathu La. Part of the historic Silk Road; closed after the 1962 India–China war, reopened for limited trade in 2006.

08The longest non-polar glacier in India is —
  1. Gangotri
  2. Siachen
  3. Zemu
  4. Pindari
Reveal answer

B — Siachen (≈ 76 km). It feeds the Nubra river, which is a tributary of the Shyok (which in turn joins the Indus).

09The Eastern Hills (Purvanchal) do not include —
  1. Patkai Bum
  2. Naga Hills
  3. Khasi Hills
  4. Mizo Hills
Reveal answer

C — Khasi Hills. Khasi Hills are part of the Meghalaya Plateau — a detached block of the Peninsular Plateau, not the Himalayan system.

10The valleys between the Shivalik and the Lesser Himalayas in the western sector are called —
  1. Duars
  2. Duns
  3. Terai
  4. Bhabar
Reveal answer

B — Duns (e.g. Dehradun). Their eastern equivalents in Assam–WB are called Duars. Terai and Bhabar are belts at the foot of the Shivalik, on the plains side — we'll meet them in Chapter 4.
