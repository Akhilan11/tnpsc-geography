CHAPTER 09 / CLIMATE · THE INDIAN MONSOON

Climate of India.

India's climate is the monsoon — a sea-breeze writ continental, ancient enough to have shaped the country's languages, agriculture, and history.

OBJECTIVES OF THIS CHAPTER

  • Identify the factors that determine India's climate (six latitudinal, four others).
  • Explain the physical mechanism of the Indian monsoon.
  • Describe the four seasons in detail.
  • Distinguish the southwest monsoon (June–Sept) from the northeast monsoon (Oct–Dec).
  • Understand El Niño, La Niña, and the Indian Ocean Dipole and how they affect Indian rainfall.
  • Recognise the Koppen climatic classes within India.

India's climate is described as tropical monsoon. The word monsoon comes from the Arabic mausim, meaning "season" — a reference to the seasonal reversal of wind direction. For half the year, winds blow from sea to land, bringing rain; for the other half, they blow from land to sea, bringing dry, cool air. The Indian agricultural cycle, the financial year, traditional festivals, even the country's water budget — all are calibrated to this annual rhythm. A single bad monsoon can move the entire GDP. This chapter is therefore one of the most economically consequential in the syllabus.

Factors determining India's climate.

Ten factors govern Indian climate. The first six are latitudinal/locational (don't change); the rest are dynamic (vary year to year).

1. Latitude

  • Tropic of Cancer divides India into tropical south (perennial warmth) and subtropical north (winter).
  • Solar insolation is fairly uniform across India year-round.

2. The Himalayan wall

  • Blocks cold Central Asian winds.
  • Forces moisture-bearing SW monsoon winds to rise and release rain.
  • Without it, north India would be desert.

3. Distance from sea

  • Coastal regions: equable climate (e.g. Chennai, Mumbai).
  • Interior: extreme — hot summers, cold winters (Delhi, Nagpur).

4. Altitude

  • Temperature falls 6.5°C per km of altitude.
  • Hill stations have temperate climates within otherwise tropical regions.

5. Pressure & winds

  • Summer low over NW India draws winds inland (SW monsoon).
  • Winter high over the same region pushes winds out (NE monsoon).

6. Ocean currents

  • Cold currents reduce rainfall; warm currents increase it.
  • The South Equatorial Current keeps the southern coasts warm.

7. Jet streams

  • Subtropical Westerly Jet over Tibet in winter brings W disturbances.
  • Tropical Easterly Jet (TEJ) over India in summer (June–Aug) is essential for a good SW monsoon.

8. Western Disturbances

  • Extra-tropical storms from the Mediterranean, steered by the Westerly Jet.
  • Bring winter rain to north India and snow to the Himalayas. Critical for rabi crops.

9. El Niño / La Niña (ENSO)

  • Pacific Ocean phenomenon affecting global weather.
  • El Niño years usually mean weak Indian monsoons.
  • La Niña years usually mean strong Indian monsoons.

10. Indian Ocean Dipole (IOD)

  • Sea-surface temperature contrast between western (Africa) and eastern (Indonesia) Indian Ocean.
  • Positive IOD = good monsoon, negative = poor.

The mechanism of the Indian monsoon.

The classic textbook explanation: in summer, the Indian landmass heats up faster than the surrounding oceans. A persistent low-pressure area builds over the Thar Desert and Punjab. Air over the cooler Indian Ocean (high pressure) flows toward this low. As the wind crosses the equator and enters the Northern Hemisphere, the Coriolis force deflects it to its right, turning the south-east trade winds into south-west monsoon winds. These pick up moisture from the Bay of Bengal and the Arabian Sea, and discharge it over India when they hit topographic barriers — the Western Ghats, the Himalayas, the Khasi Hills.

The modern understanding adds two more pieces: the role of the Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ), which shifts northward in summer, drawing winds across the equator; and the Tibetan Plateau, which acts as a high-altitude heat source, intensifying the upper-atmosphere circulation that helps drive the surface monsoon.

KEY FACT — RAINFALL DISTRIBUTION

India's annual mean rainfall is ~ 1,170 mm. About 75% of all rainfall comes from the SW monsoon (June–September). The next ~ 13% comes from the NE monsoon (Oct–Dec) — mostly to Tamil Nadu and adjoining areas. The wettest place is Mawsynram (Meghalaya, ~ 11,872 mm/yr); the driest is the Thar desert (< 150 mm/yr).

The four seasons.

The India Meteorological Department recognises four seasons:

Hot weather season (summer)Mar – MayIncreasingly hot; "loo" winds in NW; mango showers in S.
South-West monsoon (advancing)Jun – SepMajor rainy season; the principal monsoon.
Retreating / Post-monsoonOct – DecNE monsoon over TN; cyclones over Bay of Bengal.

Winter (Dec–Feb)

The sun is far south. North India experiences mean temperatures below 20°C. Punjab and J&K can drop near freezing; Himachal sees snow. Western disturbances — low-pressure systems originating over the Mediterranean — move eastward and bring 25 to 50 mm of rain (and snow) to north India. This rain is vital for rabi crops (wheat, mustard, gram). Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and parts of southern AP receive the tail of the NE monsoon.

Summer (March–May)

Temperatures rise. By May, mean daily max in much of north and central India exceeds 40°C; some interior cities (Phalodi, Churu, Nagpur, Jhansi) cross 48°C. The most striking features:

  • Loo — hot, dry, dusty wind that blows over northern India.
  • Kal Baisakhi / Norwesters — violent local storms over WB & Assam in April–May. Bring evening thundershowers; useful for tea, jute, paddy.
  • Mango showers — pre-monsoon thunderstorms over Karnataka and Kerala in April–May; help the mango harvest.
  • Cherry blossom — similar showers over Karnataka aiding coffee.

SW Monsoon — see next section.

Retreating monsoon (Oct–Dec)

The monsoon retreats from the north (around Sept 1) southward, reaching Tamil Nadu by mid-October. The reversed wind pattern picks up moisture from the Bay of Bengal and dumps it on the Coromandel coast — this is the NE monsoon. The Bay of Bengal in this season produces severe tropical cyclones, often making landfall on the Tamil Nadu or AP coast.

The Southwest Monsoon (Jun–Sep).

The principal rainy season — accounts for 75% of India's annual rainfall. The SW monsoon splits into two branches:

Arabian Sea branch

  • Strikes the Western Ghats first (Kerala).
  • Reaches Kerala around June 1 (official "burst").
  • Drenches the windward face of the Ghats; the leeward Deccan plateau sits in rain shadow.
  • Then advances north along the west coast; reaches Mumbai by June 10.

Bay of Bengal branch

  • Strikes the Andaman & Nicobar around mid-May.
  • Hits the Eastern Himalayas at the head of the Bay, gets deflected westward by the Himalayan wall.
  • Travels up the Ganga plain bringing rain from east to west.
  • Reaches Delhi by late June, Punjab by early July; covers the whole country by July 15.

Break in monsoon

Within the rainy season, there can be spells of dry weather of 3–10 days. Caused by shifts of the monsoon trough away from the foothills. Important for agriculture (long breaks = drought stress).

Withdrawal

Begins from NW India around September 1. By October, the SW monsoon has withdrawn entirely from north India. The "October heat" — return of warmer weather — comes briefly before winter.

The Northeast (Retreating) Monsoon.

The unique rainfall season of Tamil Nadu. As the SW monsoon retreats, the high pressure builds back over north India and the winds reverse, blowing from north-east to south-west. These winds, on their way south, pick up moisture from the Bay of Bengal and discharge it on the Coromandel coast — Tamil Nadu, southern Andhra Pradesh, southern Karnataka, parts of Kerala.

For Tamil Nadu, this is not a secondary monsoon: it is the main monsoon. TN receives about 48% of its annual rainfall from the NE monsoon (Oct–Dec), and only ~ 35% from the SW monsoon — the opposite of most of India.

TAMIL NADU CONNECTION — WHY TN IS DIFFERENT

Tamil Nadu's geography puts it in the rain shadow of the Western Ghats during the SW monsoon. The state therefore depends on the NE monsoon (Oct–Dec) for its main rainfall. Chennai's wettest month is November (avg ~ 400 mm). The TN agricultural calendar is built around the NE monsoon: paddy is transplanted October-November (samba/thaladi season).

El Niño, La Niña, IOD.

ENSO (El Niño Southern Oscillation) refers to the periodic warming (El Niño) and cooling (La Niña) of sea-surface temperatures in the central and eastern tropical Pacific. The pattern affects global weather.

  • El Niño — Pacific warm phase. Usually weakens the Indian monsoon (e.g. droughts of 2002, 2009, 2014, 2015).
  • La Niña — Pacific cold phase. Usually strengthens the Indian monsoon.
  • Neutral — Most years.

The relationship is statistical, not deterministic — there have been El Niño years with good monsoons (1997) and La Niña years with bad ones.

Indian Ocean Dipole (IOD) — the difference in sea-surface temperature between the western Indian Ocean (off Africa) and the eastern (off Sumatra). A positive IOD (warmer west) helps the Indian monsoon and can offset an El Niño year. A negative IOD reduces rainfall.

Madden-Julian Oscillation (MJO) — eastward-moving disturbance of clouds/rainfall over the tropics; its phase modulates monsoon active and break spells on intra-seasonal scales.

Climate-related hazards.

  • Floods — Assam (Brahmaputra), Bihar (Kosi), West Bengal (Damodar), Mumbai (urban), Chennai (urban + NE monsoon — 2015 floods).
  • Droughts — Bundelkhand, Marathwada, Rayalaseema, interior Karnataka.
  • Cyclones — Bay of Bengal generates 4 times more cyclones than the Arabian Sea. Famous cyclones: 1999 Odisha super cyclone, 2014 Hudhud, 2018 Gaja (TN), 2020 Amphan (WB), 2023 Biparjoy (Gujarat).
  • Heat waves — Telangana, Andhra, parts of UP, Bihar, Odisha.
  • Cloudbursts & flash floods — Himalayan states. Famous: Kedarnath (2013), Wayanad landslide (2024).
KEY FACT — CYCLONE NAMING

Cyclone names for the North Indian Ocean (Bay of Bengal + Arabian Sea) are jointly maintained by the IMD with 13 countries. India's contributions include names like Amphan, Gati, Tej, Asna. Tamil Nadu's Cyclone Gaja (2018) was named by Sri Lanka.

  • India's climate = tropical monsoon. Annual mean rainfall ~ 1,170 mm.
  • Ten factors: latitude, Himalayas, sea, altitude, pressure, currents, jet streams, Western Disturbances, ENSO, IOD.
  • Four seasons: Cold (Dec–Feb), Hot (Mar–May), SW Monsoon (Jun–Sep), Retreating (Oct–Dec).
  • SW monsoon arrives Kerala ~ June 1; covers India by July 15; withdraws from NW India ~ Sep 1.
  • SW monsoon = 75% of India's rain; NE monsoon = ~ 13% but ~ 48% of Tamil Nadu's rain.

Practice MCQs

CH 09 · 10 QUESTIONS
01The SW monsoon officially "bursts" over Kerala around —
  1. May 1
  2. June 1
  3. June 15
  4. July 1
Reveal answer

B — June 1. The IMD's normal date of monsoon onset over Kerala.

02Tamil Nadu receives most of its annual rainfall from the —
  1. SW monsoon
  2. NE monsoon
  3. Western disturbances
  4. Mango showers
Reveal answer

B — NE monsoon (Oct–Dec). Tamil Nadu's geography places it in the rain shadow of the Western Ghats during the SW monsoon.

03"Loo" refers to —
  1. Hot, dry summer winds in NW India
  2. Wet thunderstorms in Bengal
  3. Winter rainfall in Tamil Nadu
  4. Mango showers in Karnataka
Reveal answer

A — Hot, dry summer wind blowing over NW & central India in May–June.

04"Mango showers" are pre-monsoon thunderstorms over —
  1. Bengal & Assam
  2. Karnataka & Kerala
  3. Punjab & Haryana
  4. Tamil Nadu coast
Reveal answer

B — Karnataka & Kerala. They help ripen the mango harvest. Bengal storms are "Kal Baisakhi" / Norwesters.

05An El Niño event is associated with —
  1. Warming of the central & eastern tropical Pacific
  2. Cooling of the same
  3. Warming of the Indian Ocean
  4. Cooling of the Indian Ocean
Reveal answer

A — Warming of the central & eastern tropical Pacific. Often weakens the Indian monsoon.

06The wettest place in India — and one of the wettest in the world — is —
  1. Cherrapunji
  2. Mawsynram
  3. Agumbe
  4. Mahabaleshwar
Reveal answer

B — Mawsynram in Meghalaya (~ 11,872 mm/year). Cherrapunji is a very close second.

07The "Tropical Easterly Jet" is an upper-atmosphere wind that flows during —
  1. Indian winter
  2. Indian summer / SW monsoon
  3. Western disturbance episodes
  4. NE monsoon
Reveal answer

B — Indian summer / SW monsoon. A strong TEJ correlates with a good monsoon.

08"Western Disturbances" bring winter rainfall to north India and originate over the —
  1. Bay of Bengal
  2. Arabian Sea
  3. Mediterranean
  4. Pacific Ocean
Reveal answer

C — Mediterranean region. Steered eastward by the subtropical westerly jet.

09The annual mean rainfall of India is approximately —
  1. 700 mm
  2. 1,170 mm
  3. 1,600 mm
  4. 2,200 mm
Reveal answer

B — ~ 1,170 mm. Varies widely: Mawsynram > 11,000 mm; Thar < 150 mm.

10The Bay of Bengal generates approximately how many times as many cyclones as the Arabian Sea?
  1. Equal
  2. 2 times
  3. 4 times
  4. 10 times
Reveal answer

C — about 4 times. The Bay is warmer, shallower, and has converging coastlines that funnel storms.
